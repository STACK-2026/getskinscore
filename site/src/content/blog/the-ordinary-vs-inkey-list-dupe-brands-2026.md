**The Ordinary and The Inkey List are the two brands that made proper actives affordable**, but they are not identical twins. The Ordinary sells a wider catalogue with a chemistry forward tone. The Inkey List sells a tighter range with plain language usage guides. On the shelves that overlap, niacinamide, hyaluronic acid, salicylic acid, both perform. On retinoids and vitamin C, the picture is more nuanced. This head to head ranks them by formulation, evidence and everyday usability.

> **TL;DR**: **The Ordinary** (Deciem, acquired by Estee Lauder in 2021) offers the biggest budget catalogue in beauty, with strong niacinamide, retinoids and multiple vitamin C options. **The Inkey List** (2018, founded by Colette Laxton and Mark Curry) is smaller, warmer in tone and easier for beginners, with a very good caffeine eye cream and a friendly PHA toner. On core commodities like **niacinamide 10%**, hyaluronic acid and salicylic acid 2%, they are effectively interchangeable. The Ordinary wins depth and retinoid range. The Inkey List wins clarity and onboarding. Neither is a scam. Pick by shopping style, not by hype.

Affordable skincare stopped being an oxymoron the day The Ordinary launched a niacinamide serum for less than a lunch. Six years later, The Inkey List showed the category could feel warmer and easier to navigate. This guide walks the two catalogues side by side, active by active, and settles the choice with a rule: buy by evidence and formulation, not by brand loyalty. For where either brand fits a full routine, our [complete The Ordinary guide](/blog/the-ordinary-complete-guide-best-products/) covers the deeper catalogue, and our [ingredient encyclopedia](/encyclopedia) records the concentrations behind every claim.

## Two brands, one mission, different personalities

Both brands share a founding thesis: separate the active from the packaging, and sell the ingredient at the price the ingredient actually costs. That is where the similarity ends.

The Ordinary launched in 2013 under Canadian parent Deciem, founded by the late Brandon Truaxe. It became the loudest voice in the ingredient transparency movement, printing INCI percentages on the front label. Estee Lauder took a majority stake in Deciem in 2021, anchoring the brand inside one of the largest beauty groups in the world. Pricing has stayed low and the catalogue has grown past sixty single ingredient formulas.

The Inkey List launched in 2018 in London, founded by ex retail buyers Colette Laxton and Mark Curry. Their pitch was gentler: same low prices, but with usage instructions on every box, a warmer tone and a much shorter, curated line. Where The Ordinary treats you as a chemist, The Inkey List treats you as a first time buyer who deserves clear guidance. The brand remains independent and sits on shelves at Sephora, Boots and most large European pharmacies.

Same value proposition, opposite personalities. The right one for you depends less on the bottle and more on how you like to shop.

## Head to head: niacinamide serums

Both brands sell a 10% niacinamide serum, and both do it well.

The Ordinary Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1% pairs 10% niacinamide with 1% zinc PCA in a light, water based gel. The Inkey List Niacinamide matches the 10% headline in a slightly silkier vehicle, without the zinc, and adds hyaluronic acid for slip.

Efficacy at 10% niacinamide is well established. A double blind study in the *International Journal of Cosmetic Science* showed a 4 week topical 5% niacinamide reduced yellowing, hyperpigmented spots and red blotchiness while improving elasticity ([Bissett et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/18492135/)). The American Academy of Dermatology lists niacinamide as a supporting ingredient for barrier repair, brightening and rosacea friendly routines.

Tiebreaker: The Ordinary's version can pill under certain sunscreens because of the zinc PCA, and can sting sensitised skin. The Inkey List's glides more easily and is safer for reactive skin. For the deeper case, see our review of whether [niacinamide is overhyped](/blog/niacinamide-overhyped-truth-dermatologists-2026/). Verdict: draw. Buy by feel.

## Head to head: retinol and retinoids

This is where The Ordinary pulls ahead on range.

The Ordinary sells four main retinoid options: Retinol 0.2%, 0.5% and 1% in Squalane, plus a Granactive Retinoid 2% Emulsion. That ladder lets you climb from a barely there dose to a strong one at the same low price. The Inkey List sells one retinol serum at 1%, plus a bakuchiol option for those avoiding retinoids entirely.

Retinoids remain the best studied topical anti ageing family. A landmark trial in *Archives of Dermatology* showed measurable improvement in fine wrinkles, tactile roughness and pigmentation with topical retinol over 24 weeks ([Kafi et al., 2007](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17515510/)). The American Academy of Dermatology reiterates that retinoid families remain first line for acne and photoaging in its [acne clinical guideline](https://www.aad.org/member/clinical-quality/guidelines/acne). Our review of the [best anti ageing ingredients ranked by evidence](/blog/best-anti-aging-ingredients-ranked-evidence/) puts them in wider context.

The Ordinary wins because a beginner can start at 0.2% and climb without changing brand. The Inkey List's single 1% is fine for experienced users but not the safest entry point. Verdict: The Ordinary.

## Head to head: vitamin C

The Ordinary wins depth; The Inkey List wins simplicity.

The Ordinary offers a full ladder: L Ascorbic Acid 8% + Alpha Arbutin 2%, Ascorbyl Glucoside 12%, Ethylated Ascorbic Acid 15%, Ascorbyl Tetraisopalmitate 20%, and the classic Vitamin C Suspension 23%. Five different chemistries, each with its own stability and sensory profile. Some are excellent (Ascorbyl Glucoside 12%), some are hard to use (the gritty 23% suspension).

The Inkey List sells C-50, a 15% ethylated ascorbic acid serum with a stable derivative, and that is essentially it. A reliable, non irritating, no grit choice, but one option not five.

If you know your vitamin C chemistry, The Ordinary gives you every legitimate flavour. If you just want a working serum, The Inkey List's C-50 is easier. See our [vitamin C serum guide covering forms and concentrations](/blog/vitamin-c-serum-guide-forms-concentrations/). Verdict: The Ordinary for depth, The Inkey List for beginners.

## Head to head: hyaluronic acid

Draw.

The Ordinary Hyaluronic Acid 2% + B5 uses low, medium and high molecular weight HA plus panthenol. The Inkey List Hyaluronic Acid Serum uses a similar blend with a slightly lighter finish. Both are 2%, both deliver measurable short term hydration, and neither is a moisturiser replacement. No meaningful clinical daylight between them.

Hyaluronic acid needs humidity or a moisturiser on top to work well; dry climate users often report tightness after either serum. Our note on the [hyaluronic acid mistakes almost everyone makes](/blog/hyaluronic-acid-mistakes-everyone-makes/) covers the humidity trap. Verdict: total draw.

## Head to head: salicylic acid and exfoliants

A draw, with a small edge to The Ordinary on range.

The Ordinary sells Salicylic Acid 2% Solution, an Anhydrous version, the AHA 30% + BHA 2% Peeling Solution and a Glycolic Acid 7% Toner. The Inkey List sells a BHA (salicylic acid 2%) exfoliant and a PHA toner. Both salicylic options work at 2%, the concentration most trial evidence supports for mild to moderate acne.

The Ordinary's Peeling Solution is a weekly ritual product The Inkey List does not directly rival. For how to choose between acid families, see our comparison of [salicylic acid versus glycolic acid](/blog/salicylic-acid-vs-glycolic-acid-bha-aha-2026/). Verdict: The Ordinary for range, either brand is fine for everyday BHA.

## Head to head: peptides, caffeine and eye care

The Inkey List finally wins one.

The Ordinary Caffeine Solution 5% + EGCG is a fluid serum for under eye puffiness, not a moisturiser. The Inkey List Caffeine Eye Cream pairs caffeine with a proper emollient base, delivering hydration and a smoothing finish at once, which most users prefer for mornings before makeup.

On peptides, both brands sell something. The Ordinary offers Multi Peptide + HA Serum and Argireline Solution 10%. The Inkey List sells a peptide moisturiser with a friendlier texture. Peptides deliver modest results on wrinkles and firmness, and neither brand's version is a game changer.

If eyes are your priority, that one Inkey List cream can tip the whole brand decision. Verdict: The Inkey List, narrowly.

## Formulation, packaging and labelling

Two very different shopping experiences.

The Ordinary packages nearly everything in identical dropper bottles with tiny minimalist labels. Clean on Instagram, a nightmare in a real bathroom cabinet at 6 a.m. The website has become the primary manual, not the packaging.

The Inkey List packages products in boxes that print a plain language usage guide on the back: what it does, when to use it, what to layer it with. For a first time active user that alone is worth the small price difference. The website runs a free "Ask INKEY" tool with human answers. This is the strongest advantage the brand holds.

Both brands are cruelty free and offer fragrance free options across most of the range. Both meet European labelling requirements documented on the [European Commission's cosmetics regulation portal](https://health.ec.europa.eu/cosmetic-products_en).

## Where each brand loses

Both brands have blind spots.

The Ordinary's weaknesses are usability: fragile dropper packaging, a gritty Vitamin C Suspension 23%, retinoids that oxidise quickly once open, and a sheer catalogue size that pushes buyers into over stacking, one of the most common mistakes we see in our [SkinScore rankings](/rankings) analysis.

The Inkey List's weaknesses are range: no full retinoid ladder, no strong home peel option, fewer specialised actives. Its 1% retinol is not the gentlest starting point either.

Both share one blind spot: sunscreen. The Ordinary's UV line has struggled with formulation complaints; The Inkey List does not offer a sunscreen at all. Buy your SPF elsewhere.

## How they compare on price

Similar. Almost identical.

Most single active serums from both brands sit between 6 and 15 euros in Europe, and between 8 and 20 US dollars in North America. Retinoids, peptides and higher percentage vitamin C options push the top end. A common myth is that The Ordinary is dramatically cheaper; in 2026 it is not. Buy on formulation and personal fit, not on saving one euro per bottle.

## Which one should you actually buy

Buy The Ordinary if you already know what active you want, want a full retinoid ladder, or want the deepest catalogue of vitamin C forms. It suits shoppers comfortable reading INCI lists and building routines unassisted.

Buy The Inkey List if you are new to actives and want the box itself to tell you when and how to use the product. It suits shoppers who value clear usage guides, warmer branding and a strong caffeine eye cream.

Buy both if you want the best of each. A working mix: The Ordinary Niacinamide 10% + Zinc, The Ordinary Retinol 0.5% in Squalane, The Inkey List Caffeine Eye Cream, either brand's hyaluronic acid. Add a barrier friendly moisturiser and a proper sunscreen. Our guide to [ceramides and the skin barrier](/blog/ceramides-explained-why-your-barrier-needs-them/) covers the moisturiser layer, and our [skincare routine order guide](/blog/skincare-routine-order-morning-night/) sequences it all.

Whichever brand wins your shelf, formulation beats brand loyalty and consistency over three months matters more than any single serum.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is The Ordinary or The Inkey List better for beginners?

The Inkey List is easier for beginners. Every box prints plain language usage instructions, the tone is warmer, and the range is smaller so the choice is less overwhelming. The Ordinary is better once you know what active you want and how to build a routine yourself. If you have never used actives before, start with The Inkey List, then explore The Ordinary once your confidence grows.

### Are The Ordinary and The Inkey List actually different brands?

Yes, fully separate companies. The Ordinary is owned by Deciem, whose majority stake was acquired by Estee Lauder in 2021. The Inkey List was founded independently in London in 2018 by Colette Laxton and Mark Curry, and remains independent. They share a business model, low priced single ingredient serums, but the ownership, teams and formulations are distinct.

### Are the niacinamide serums from both brands the same?

Almost. Both use 10% niacinamide, the top of the useful cosmetic range. The Ordinary adds 1% zinc PCA, which some skin types find slightly astringent and which can occasionally pill under sunscreen. The Inkey List's version is silkier and better for sensitive skin. Efficacy is comparable at the label percentage; the difference is texture, not result.

### Which brand has better retinol?

The Ordinary, because of range. It sells retinol in squalane at 0.2%, 0.5% and 1%, plus a Granactive Retinoid 2% Emulsion, which lets you climb from a starter dose to a strong one at the same price point. The Inkey List sells one retinol at 1%, which is fine for experienced users but too strong as a first step. If you are new to retinoids, start with The Ordinary Retinol 0.2% in Squalane and follow the American Academy of Dermatology's cautions on gradual introduction and daily SPF.

### Can I mix products from both brands in one routine?

Yes, and many people do. The formulas are cosmetic strength and generally compatible. The usual layering rules apply: do not stack strong acids with retinoids the same night, keep to two or three actives per session, and give any new product two to three weeks before adding another. A common working combination is The Ordinary Niacinamide, The Inkey List Caffeine Eye Cream and either brand's hyaluronic acid, layered under a proper moisturiser and daily sunscreen.

## The bottom line

The Ordinary and The Inkey List are not rivals in the melodramatic sense. They are two versions of the same idea, aimed at slightly different shoppers. The Ordinary is deeper, more clinical in tone, and unmatched for a retinoid or vitamin C ladder. The Inkey List is warmer, easier for beginners, and holds a genuine edge on eye cream. On the commodity actives that most people actually need, niacinamide, hyaluronic acid and a salicylic acid exfoliant, they are essentially interchangeable. Buy by shopping style and formulation fit, layer smart, protect the barrier, wear sunscreen, and either brand will earn its place in your routine.
