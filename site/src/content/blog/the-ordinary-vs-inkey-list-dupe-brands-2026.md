**The Ordinary vs The Inkey List**: The Ordinary wins on the widest active range, the lowest price per active and precise concentration labelling, which rewards people who already read INCI lists. The Inkey List wins on plain language names, simpler routines and gentler onboarding for beginners, at a slightly higher but still low price. Both are transparent, affordable brands. The deciding factor is whether you want to engineer your own routine or be guided through one.

> **TL;DR**: Pick **The Ordinary** if you already understand actives, want the cheapest possible price per ingredient, and like choosing your own concentrations. Pick **The Inkey List** if you are newer to skincare, prefer product names in plain English, and want a brand that tells you what to use for a concern instead of listing molecules. Both sell most single-active products under 15 EUR. The Ordinary has more depth. The Inkey List has less friction.

Both brands built the same business on the same insight: skincare actives are cheap to formulate, so the markup on a luxury serum is mostly marketing. Strip that out, print the concentration on the bottle, and sell a niacinamide serum for the price of a coffee. That is the entire affordable-actives category in one sentence, and The Ordinary and The Inkey List are the two brands that own it.

But they are not interchangeable. One is a chemistry set. The other is a cookbook. This guide compares them the way a routine audit would: pricing, ingredient transparency, the actual product lineup, how gentle each is on beginners, and which specific products from each brand are the ones worth buying. Every product mentioned is graded on [SkinScore](/rankings).

## The Ordinary vs The Inkey List: two philosophies, one price point

The Ordinary is the affordable-actives brand from Deciem, launched in 2016 with a deliberately clinical identity. The names are the formulas: Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1%, Hyaluronic Acid 2% + B5, Glycolic Acid 7% Toning Solution. There is no storytelling, no concern-based branding on the front label, and no attempt to hide what the product is. The brand assumes you already know what niacinamide does and just wants to sell it to you at cost plus a small margin.

The Inkey List launched in 2018 with the same low-price thesis but the opposite communication strategy. Product names are still ingredient-led, but the brand wraps each one in plain-English guidance: what it does, when to use it, what to pair it with, and what to avoid. The packaging carries a mini routine map. Where The Ordinary hands you a molecule, The Inkey List hands you an instruction.

That single difference explains almost everything downstream. The Ordinary's catalogue is deeper and more granular because it is designed for people who want options. The Inkey List's catalogue is tighter and more curated because it is designed to prevent decision paralysis. Neither approach is wrong. They serve different users at different stages.

## Pricing: which dupe brand is actually cheaper

The Ordinary is usually cheaper per active, and the gap is real rather than symbolic. Most single-active The Ordinary products sit between 6 and 12 EUR, while comparable Inkey List products sit between 8 and 15 EUR. On a like-for-like basis, a full The Ordinary routine of cleanser, niacinamide serum, hyaluronic acid serum and a moisturiser will usually undercut the equivalent Inkey List basket.

The [The Ordinary Niacinamide 10% + Zinc 1% serum](https://theordinary.com/en-us/niacinamide-10-zinc-1-serum-100436.html) is the clearest example, a 30ml bottle of a 10% niacinamide formula at a price that made the whole category possible. The [The Inkey List Niacinamide serum](https://theinkeylist.com/products/niacinamide) sits at a similar 10% concentration but typically costs a euro or two more.

The caveat is that cheapest per active is not the same as cheapest per outcome. The Ordinary's granularity means it is easy to overbuy, ending up with eight bottles you rotate badly. The Inkey List's curation means you buy fewer products and are more likely to actually finish them. For a disciplined ingredient shopper, The Ordinary wins on price. For someone prone to buying half a chemistry set and using none of it, The Inkey List's simplicity can be the cheaper path in practice.

## Ingredient transparency: both are strong, one is more precise

Both brands are far more transparent than mainstream cosmetic ranges, but The Ordinary is the more precise of the two. The Ordinary prints exact concentrations on the front of nearly every product, which is rare in the industry and genuinely useful when you are trying to avoid stacking two strong acids or doubling up on the same active. If you can read a percentage, you can predict how a The Ordinary product will behave.

The Inkey List lists ingredients and headline concentrations too, but leans more on the plain-language claim than the exact number. A newcomer benefits from that. Someone building a careful routine around known percentages sometimes has to dig a little further to find the precise figure. Both brands publish full INCI lists, which is the baseline any credible affordable brand should meet. If you are new to reading those lists, our [how to read an INCI list guide](/blog/how-to-read-inci-list-skincare/) is a five-minute primer that makes both brands easier to shop.

Transparency matters because it lets the peer-reviewed evidence do the talking. Niacinamide, the flagship active for both brands, is one of the best-studied cosmetic ingredients. A clinical study published in the [British Journal of Dermatology found 2% topical niacinamide significantly reduced sebum excretion rates across trials in 100 Japanese and 30 Caucasian subjects](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16766489/), which is why both brands position their niacinamide products at oily and acne-prone skin. Separately, research on niacinamide and pigmentation, tested at 5% in [18 subjects with facial hyperpigmentation](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12100180/), found it reduced pigment by suppressing melanosome transfer from melanocytes to keratinocytes. When a brand prints the concentration, you can match the bottle to the study.

## Product lineup: depth versus curation

The Ordinary's range is the deeper of the two by a wide margin. Beyond niacinamide and hyaluronic acid it carries a full acids library (glycolic, lactic, salicylic, mandelic, azelaic), multiple vitamin C formats, several retinoid strengths, peptides, and a growing set of targeted treatments. The [The Ordinary Azelaic Acid Suspension 10%](https://theordinary.com/en-us/azelaic-acid-suspension-10-100425.html) and the [Granactive Retinoid 2% Emulsion](https://theordinary.com/en-us/granactive-retinoid-2-emulsion-100427.html) are examples of niche actives that most affordable brands do not touch, offered at prices that undercut prescription-adjacent alternatives.

The Inkey List's range is narrower and more curated. It covers the essentials and the popular actives but deliberately avoids the long tail of obscure formulations. For most people this is a feature, not a limitation. You are unlikely to need six exfoliating acids, and the shorter menu makes it far easier to build a coherent routine without accidentally stacking incompatible products.

If your concern is one specific active, both brands usually carry it. If your concern is building a full multi-step routine from a single low-cost brand, The Ordinary gives you more to work with, while The Inkey List keeps you from overcomplicating it. ## Beginner friendliness: where The Inkey List pulls ahead

The Inkey List is the clearly better brand for a beginner, and this is by design rather than accident. The plain-language naming, the on-pack routine guidance and the tighter catalogue all reduce the number of decisions a newcomer has to make. When you do not yet know the difference between a BHA and an AHA, being handed a curated shortlist with instructions is far safer than being handed a wall of numbered percentages.

The Ordinary, for all its transparency, can overwhelm a beginner. The clinical naming assumes prior knowledge, the sheer number of similar-looking products invites mistakes, and the brand does relatively little to stop you buying two exfoliants and using them on the same night. A confident ingredient shopper reads that as freedom. A nervous newcomer reads it as risk. The most common early mistake with The Ordinary is over-exfoliation from stacking acids, which our guide on [hyaluronic acid mistakes everyone makes](/blog/hyaluronic-acid-mistakes-everyone-makes/) touches on in the context of layering.

If you are just starting, The Inkey List's guardrails are worth the small price premium. If you already understand actives and want maximum control, The Ordinary's lack of guardrails is exactly the point.

## Formulation feel and texture

Both brands prioritise function over sensory experience, but there are real texture differences worth knowing. The Ordinary's formulas can be minimalist to a fault. Some of its hyaluronic acid and acid products have a slightly tacky or drag finish because the formula is stripped of the silicones and slip agents that a pricier serum would include. The [The Ordinary Hyaluronic Acid 2% + B5](https://theordinary.com/en-us/hyaluronic-acid-2-b5-hydrating-serum-100418.html) is effective but can feel sticky under other layers if you apply too much.

The Inkey List tends to invest a little more in the sensory side, with textures that feel slightly more finished and easier to layer. This is part of the beginner-friendly positioning. A product that feels pleasant is a product a newcomer keeps using. Neither brand reaches the polish of a luxury serum, but The Inkey List usually feels the more refined of the two on the skin.

Texture is not a trivial detail. Adherence to a routine is the single biggest predictor of results, and a formula you enjoy applying is a formula you will actually use. For sensitive or reactive skin, a smoother texture with fewer sensory triggers also lowers the odds of a reaction. ## Availability, restocks and buying without regret

Both brands are widely available, but the buying experience differs in ways that matter over a year of use. The Ordinary is stocked in more places, from its parent retailer to most large beauty chains and pharmacies, which makes replacing a finished bottle easy in most countries. Its popularity has a downside: hero products like the niacinamide serum periodically sell out during viral spikes, and regional pricing can vary more than you would expect for a budget brand.

The Inkey List has narrower distribution in some markets but a more predictable stock pattern, partly because its tighter catalogue means fewer individual SKUs to keep on shelves. If you build a routine around three or four Inkey List products, you are less likely to hit an out-of-stock wall than someone relying on a specific The Ordinary formulation that goes viral overnight.

The smarter buying strategy with either brand is to resist the low prices. Because a single active costs less than a sandwich, it is tempting to fill a basket with ten products at once. Do not. Buy one or two actives, introduce them one at a time over several weeks, and only add the next once your skin has adapted. This is the single most useful habit for both brands, and it matters more with The Ordinary because the sheer size of the range makes over-buying so easy. A patient, one-active-at-a-time approach turns two cheap brands into a genuinely effective routine instead of a shelf of half-used bottles.

## Which one should you buy?

Pick **The Ordinary** if:

- You already understand actives and want to build a precise, ingredient-led routine.
- You want the lowest possible price per active and do not need hand-holding.
- You want access to niche actives like azelaic acid, mandelic acid or multiple retinoid formats.
- You like choosing your own concentrations and reading the exact percentage on the bottle.

Pick **The Inkey List** if:

- You are newer to skincare and want clear guidance instead of a wall of percentages.
- You prefer a tighter, curated catalogue that prevents decision paralysis.
- You want slightly nicer textures and on-pack routine advice.
- You would rather buy four products you understand than ten you do not.

You can also mix, and plenty of people do. A common approach is to use The Inkey List for the actives you are unsure about, because the guidance lowers the risk, and The Ordinary for the actives you already know you want at the lowest possible price. There is no loyalty required. Both brands are cheap enough that owning products from each is normal, and the ingredient lists are compatible across brands.

Whichever you choose, track how your skin responds so you can tell which specific product actually helped rather than which one you assumed helped. Our [encyclopedia](/encyclopedia) and [ranking pages](/rankings) are built for exactly this kind of like-for-like comparison across affordable brands.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is The Ordinary or The Inkey List better for beginners?

The Inkey List is the better beginner brand. Its plain-language product names, on-pack routine guidance and smaller, curated catalogue make it far harder to build a bad routine by accident. The Ordinary assumes you already understand actives, so it gives you more freedom and more ways to make a mistake. Start with The Inkey List, then graduate to The Ordinary once you know what your skin needs.

### Which brand is cheaper, The Ordinary or The Inkey List?

The Ordinary is usually cheaper per active. Most single-active products sit a euro or two below the equivalent Inkey List product. But cheapest per bottle is not always cheapest per outcome. The Ordinary's depth makes it easy to overbuy products you never finish, so a disciplined Inkey List basket can cost less in practice for someone prone to impulse buying.

### Are The Ordinary and The Inkey List actually good, or just cheap?

Both are genuinely good, not just cheap. They use the same well-studied actives found in far pricier serums, and both publish full ingredient lists and headline concentrations. The peer-reviewed evidence behind ingredients like niacinamide and hyaluronic acid applies to these formulas just as it does to luxury versions. The main thing you give up at this price is sensory polish, not efficacy.

### Can I use The Ordinary and The Inkey List together in one routine?

Yes. The formulas are compatible across brands, so you can cleanse with one, use a serum from the other, and moisturise with either. The usual caution applies to layering multiple actives, especially acids and retinoids, regardless of which brand they come from. Avoid stacking two exfoliants or an acid with a retinoid on the same night unless your skin is already used to both.

### Which brand has the wider range of actives?

The Ordinary has the deeper catalogue by a wide margin. It carries multiple acids, several vitamin C formats, several retinoid strengths, peptides and niche actives like azelaic acid that most affordable brands do not offer. The Inkey List deliberately keeps its range tighter and more curated, which is better for avoiding overwhelm but gives you fewer options for building a complex routine.

## The bottom line

The Ordinary is a chemistry set. The Inkey List is a cookbook. If you already read INCI lists, want the lowest price per active and like engineering your own routine, The Ordinary is the honest answer. If you are newer, want guidance instead of a molecule menu, and would rather finish four products than hoard ten, The Inkey List earns its small premium.

Neither brand is a compromise. They are two solutions to the same problem, aimed at two different users. Buy the one that matches how much control you actually want, not the one a viral video told you to.
