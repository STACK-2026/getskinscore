**The Ordinary vs The Inkey List**: The Ordinary wins for raw high concentration actives and rock bottom prices, while The Inkey List wins for beginner friendly blended formulas that are easier to layer without irritation. Both brands sell transparent, dermatologist compatible skincare well under 15 euros a bottle, so the real question is whether you want to build a routine from single actives or from pre balanced blends. The deciding factor is your experience level, not the brand name.

> **TL;DR**: **The Ordinary** is the Deciem owned budget lab: single ingredient formulas at high concentrations, minimal price markup, INCI lists you can decode in seconds, best if you already understand actives. **The Inkey List** is the UK challenger built around blended, plain English formulas: actives are mixed with soothing bases and clear instructions, best if you are new to skincare or have reactive skin. The Ordinary is cheaper per active. The Inkey List is friendlier per bottle. Both are cruelty free, both are fragrance free in most core lines, and both are safe when used sensibly with sunscreen. Pick by how much you want the formula to hold your hand.

The Ordinary and The Inkey List broke the myth that cheap skincare has to be bad skincare. They sit on the same shelf at almost the same price, both promising serious actives without the ten times markup. Underneath, they are aimed at very different shoppers: The Ordinary treats you like a chemist, The Inkey List like a friend at the pharmacy counter. This guide compares them on formulation, evidence and skin type fit, with sources you can check.

## What each brand is built around

The Ordinary is a single active laboratory, and The Inkey List is a blended formula brand.

The Ordinary was launched in 2016 by Deciem in Toronto with a provocative pitch: sell raw actives at high concentrations, name each product by its INCI ingredient and its percentage, and remove the marketing markup. A bottle of Niacinamide 10% plus Zinc 1% costs less than a coffee. It does not blend or soften, and it assumes you already know which acid or vitamin your skin needs.

The Inkey List launched in the United Kingdom in 2018 with the opposite bet: blended formulas built around one hero active, mixed with soothing agents and humectants, plus plain English descriptions and how to cards. Where The Ordinary hands you a scalpel, The Inkey List hands you a pre wrapped kit. The bottles are still cheap, but you pay a small premium for the guidance.

## Niacinamide: the flagship comparison

Both brands sell a hero niacinamide serum, and the two products explain the whole brand philosophy in one bottle.

The Ordinary's Niacinamide 10% plus Zinc 1% delivers exactly what the name promises. Niacinamide is one of the best studied cosmetic actives: it reduces sebum production, calms inflammation, helps fade post inflammatory hyperpigmentation, and in cultured human keratinocytes it increased ceramide biosynthesis by 4.1 to 5.5 fold over six days while lowering transepidermal water loss ([Tanno et al., 2000](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10971324/)). At 10%, this is the upper end of what routine cosmetic use targets, at a comically low price.

The Inkey List's Niacinamide serum sits at a more moderate concentration and blends it with hyaluronic acid in a lightweight base. It is less potent per drop, but better tolerated for reactive or dehydrated skin, and it layers under sunscreen without tackiness. If your barrier is fragile, or if a 10% concentration has caused tightness or flushing, the blended version is the smarter start.

## Acids: exfoliation done two very different ways

The Ordinary sells chemical exfoliants at concentrations that are close to professional peels, and The Inkey List sells blends aimed at daily gentle use. Both approaches work, but they work for different users.

The Ordinary's AHA 30% plus BHA 2% Peeling Solution is a ten minute weekly mask at concentrations most cosmetic exfoliants stay well below. Glycolic acid has strong evidence for improving photodamage, pigmentation and texture at professional concentrations, and lower concentrations show clinical benefit for acne and post inflammatory hyperpigmentation ([Sharad, 2013](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24399857/)). Salicylic acid is lipophilic, penetrates sebum filled pores and is a first line active for acne, recommended by the American Academy of Dermatology as a core over the counter option for mild acne ([AAD acne guidelines](https://www.aad.org/public/diseases/acne/derm-treat/treat)). At 30% AHA, the Peeling Solution is effective and easy to overuse, which is why it carries strong warnings.

The Inkey List's exfoliants take the opposite approach. Its glycolic acid toner sits at a routine friendly concentration in a hydrating base, and its salicylic acid cleanser washes off after a short contact time. Nothing is heroic, and nothing is easy to burn yourself with. For a beginner, that safety margin is worth the mild premium.

If you have never used chemical exfoliation, start with the blended options and work up. Our detailed [salicylic acid versus glycolic acid guide](/blog/salicylic-acid-vs-glycolic-acid-bha-aha-2026/) explains which one your skin type actually wants.

## Vitamin C: where formulation matters most

Neither brand fully solves the vitamin C stability problem, but their attempts show how they differ. The Ordinary offers many vitamin C formats. The Inkey List offers fewer but more approachable ones.

Pure L ascorbic acid is the most studied form of vitamin C and the reference for antioxidant, brightening and collagen supporting effects, but it is famously unstable in water based formulas and turns brown as it oxidises ([Al-Niaimi and Chiang, 2017](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/28979664/)). The Ordinary's approach is to give you many options: a silicone based suspension of L ascorbic acid, a water based ethylated ascorbic acid, and several other forms. The upside is choice. The downside is that some textures are gritty or greasy, and picking between them without guidance is confusing.

The Inkey List simplifies the shelf. It offers a stabilised vitamin C serum in a lighter emulsion designed to layer under sunscreen without pilling. Concentrations are moderate, textures are pleasant and the packaging is opaque. You do not get the highest L ascorbic acid concentration, but you get a formula you will actually use every morning, which for vitamin C matters more than raw percentage.

## Retinoids: the biggest gap

Retinoids are where the two brands diverge the most, and where reading the fine print matters most.

The Ordinary sells retinol at multiple concentrations and also sells retinal (retinaldehyde), which sits one step closer to retinoic acid in the skin's conversion pathway. A left cheek and right cheek study of a 0.05% retinaldehyde cream in photoaged skin showed measurable improvement over 18 weeks ([Creidi et al., 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9737771/)). Deciem's price for a stable retinal formula is a fraction of what boutique brands charge, which is genuinely disruptive. The trade off is that the range spans low to high strengths and does not always explain which to start with.

The Inkey List's retinoid range is deliberately shorter. Its retinol sits at a beginner friendly concentration in a blended base with soothing agents, and the marketing steers you toward frequency and layering rather than percentage bragging. This is the safer starting point for a first retinoid.

The routine matters more than the bottle. Our [retinol beginner's guide](/blog/retinol-beginners-guide-how-to-start/) and comparison of [retinol versus retinal](/blog/retinol-vs-retinal-which-works-better-2026/) explain how to start slowly and avoid the classic month one flare.

## Hydration and barrier support

Both brands sell hyaluronic acid, both sell peptides and both sell moisturisers, but the everyday feel is quite different.

The Ordinary's Hyaluronic Acid 2% plus B5 uses a mix of molecular weights, which is technically smart. Different molecular weights distribute at different depths of the stratum corneum, and combining them delivers hydration across multiple layers ([Essendoubi et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26386374/)). The texture is tackier than modern serums but works well under an occlusive moisturiser. The rest of Deciem's hydration line includes the Natural Moisturising Factors cream and peptide serums at low prices, though textures are frank rather than luxurious.

The Inkey List's hydration side leans into pleasant textures. Its hyaluronic acid serum feels lighter, its ceramide cream lands between CeraVe and a mid range brand, and its peptide serums layer smoothly under makeup. Concentrations are usually more conservative, but the daily experience is friendlier. ## Textures, packaging and daily reality

The Ordinary looks and feels like a lab, and The Inkey List looks and feels like a shopping trip, and that reality decides how many of the bottles get used past week one.

The Ordinary's packaging is clinical: brown glass dropper bottles, uniform labels, ingredient and percentage on the front. Beautiful in a functional way, and terrible for anyone who cannot remember what each dropper does at 07:00. Textures are honest: the Vitamin C Suspension 23% is gritty until it warms up, and the pure squalane is an oil rather than a serum.

The Inkey List's packaging is friendly: coloured caps, plain English descriptions and how to cards. Textures across the range are light, silicone smoothed and layer well under sunscreen. Nothing is technically superior, but the whole line is easier to live with, and adherence is what turns a cheap active into a real result. Consistency beats concentration.

## Price and value

The Ordinary is the cheaper of two cheap brands, but The Inkey List is not far behind, and the price differences are smaller than the philosophies.

Per active, The Ordinary is usually the winner. A 10% niacinamide bottle costs about the price of two coffees, and its retinoids and vitamin C options sit far below any prestige alternative. If you already know exactly which actives you need, you can build a full routine for the price of one boutique serum. The Inkey List charges a small premium for the blended base, packaging and instructions. Across a full routine, expect to pay 10 to 20% more than the Deciem equivalent.

Both are still miles cheaper than the drugstore mid range and a fraction of prestige. Our comparison of [La Mer versus CeraVe](/blog/la-mer-vs-cerave-350-euro-question/) shows how little the barrier cares about price.

## Which one should you buy?

Buy The Ordinary if you already know what your skin needs, you enjoy the ingredient side of skincare, and you want the maximum active per euro. Its price for retinal, high concentration niacinamide and acid peels is impossible to beat. It rewards patience, INCI literacy and introducing one product at a time.

Buy The Inkey List if you are newer to skincare, have reactive skin, or want a routine that gives you the answer without a lecture. Blended formulas are easier to layer, harder to over concentrate and come with clearer instructions, at a modest premium.

Plenty of people quietly use both: a Deciem niacinamide or retinal for the raw active, and an Inkey List cleanser or ceramide cream for the daily feel. Whatever you choose, a modest active used consistently with proper sunscreen will always outperform a heroic percentage used once a week. ## Frequently asked questions

### Is The Ordinary or The Inkey List better for beginners?

The Inkey List is the friendlier starting point for beginners because its formulas are blended, its concentrations are more moderate and each product comes with plain English instructions. The Ordinary is cheaper and more potent per bottle, but it assumes you already know which actives your skin needs and how to layer them. Start with The Inkey List for the first few months, and add Deciem actives once you understand your own tolerance and routine.

### Can I mix products from The Ordinary and The Inkey List in the same routine?

Yes, and many budget routines do exactly that. Both brands are transparent about their INCI lists, both are largely fragrance free in their core lines and there is no chemical reason to keep them apart. A typical mixed routine might use an Inkey List cleanser and hyaluronic acid, a Deciem niacinamide or retinal at night and a shared sunscreen on top. Introduce one product at a time so you can tell which one your skin is responding to.

### Which brand has better retinol or retinoid options?

The Ordinary has the wider retinoid range and the sharper price on advanced options like retinaldehyde, which sits one step closer to retinoic acid than retinol. The Inkey List offers a shorter, more beginner friendly line at gentler concentrations. If you have never used a retinoid, start with The Inkey List. If you already tolerate retinol and want to move up efficiently, The Ordinary is the more cost effective route. Our [retinol beginner's guide](/blog/retinol-beginners-guide-how-to-start/) explains the safe way in from either brand.

### Are The Ordinary and The Inkey List safe during pregnancy?

Both brands sell products that are not appropriate in pregnancy, notably retinoids and high strength acids, and both sell products that are commonly considered safe such as hyaluronic acid, niacinamide and gentle cleansers. The safest approach is to check every INCI list with your obstetrician or dermatologist and to avoid retinol, retinaldehyde and high concentration exfoliants during pregnancy and breastfeeding. General guidance on pregnancy safe ingredients aligns with the [American Academy of Dermatology](https://www.aad.org/) recommendations to avoid retinoids in pregnancy.

### Is the higher concentration on The Ordinary always better?

No. Higher concentration means more active per drop, not automatically better results. For barrier friendly ingredients like niacinamide, moving from 5% to 10% can raise the risk of irritation in reactive skin without adding proportional benefit. For exfoliating acids and retinoids, a concentration that is too high for your tolerance simply causes damage rather than faster results. The right concentration is the highest one your skin uses consistently without irritation. Blended, lower strength options from The Inkey List are often the smarter long term choice for reactive skin, even if the label number is smaller.

## The bottom line

The Ordinary and The Inkey List are the two proofs that skincare can be cheap and honest at the same time, and they win at opposite tasks. The Ordinary is the single active lab: highest concentrations, lowest prices, minimal blending, best for anyone who already speaks INCI. The Inkey List is the blended, beginner friendly range: gentler concentrations, better textures, clearer instructions and a small premium for the guidance. Decide by where you are on the learning curve, and know that many people quietly use both.
