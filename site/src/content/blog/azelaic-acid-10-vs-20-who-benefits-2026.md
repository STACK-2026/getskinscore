**Azelaic acid 10% is the over the counter cosmetic strength, while 15% gel and 20% cream are prescription formulas.** The gap between them is smaller than it looks. A well formulated 10% is enough for maintenance, mild breakouts and early pigmentation, and it lets sensitive skin build tolerance. The prescription strengths earn their place for papulopustular rosacea and moderate acne, where clinical trials used 15% and 20%. The right choice depends on your concern, your skin's tolerance and whether you can actually get a prescription, not on chasing the biggest number.

> **TL;DR**: **Azelaic acid 10%** is the cosmetic OTC tier: gentle, daily friendly, good for maintenance, mild acne and post acne marks. **15% gel** (Finacea) and **20% cream** (Azelex, Skinoren) are prescription strengths with the strongest trial evidence for rosacea and moderate acne. The percentage matters less than formulation quality, pH and how consistently you use it. Start at 10% if you are new, escalate only if you plateau or a doctor advises it. All three take 8 to 12 weeks, pair well with niacinamide, ceramides and SPF, and should not be layered with strong acids or a fresh retinoid start on the same night.

Azelaic acid is the ingredient dermatologists reach for when they want one active to handle acne, rosacea and dark spots at the same time, without wrecking the barrier. The most common question is not whether to use it, but at what strength. The tube on a pharmacy shelf says 10%. The gel behind the counter says 15%. The cream your dermatologist prescribes says 20%. Those numbers hide a real story about formulation, evidence and who actually benefits from more. This guide walks through the biochemistry, the clinical data with sources you can check, and a clear rule for choosing your strength.

## What azelaic acid does, regardless of strength

Azelaic acid works the same way at every concentration; the strength changes the dose, not the mechanism. It is a naturally occurring saturated dicarboxylic acid, and unlike an AHA or a BHA it does not exfoliate the surface. Its actions are biochemical, which is why it stays gentle even at 20%.

Three effects sit at the centre of its file. It is bacteriostatic against Cutibacterium acnes, the bacterium that drives inflammatory breakouts, so it lowers the load without the resistance concerns tied to topical antibiotics. It scavenges free radicals in the follicle, which calms the neutrophil driven redness common to both acne and rosacea. And it competitively inhibits tyrosinase, the enzyme that starts melanin production, which is why it fades post inflammatory marks and melasma. The classic pharmacology review in *Drugs* confirmed that topical azelaic acid, usually as a 20% cream, is effective across comedonal and inflammatory acne as well as hyperpigmentary disorders, and that it competes with tretinoin, benzoyl peroxide and hydroquinone on efficacy ([Fitton and Goa, 1991](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1712709/)).

Because it does three jobs in one tube, azelaic acid suits people whose skin has overlapping problems: a bit of acne, some redness, and the brown marks left behind when spots heal. ## The three strengths explained

There are effectively three tiers, and knowing which is which stops you overpaying or under dosing.

**10% is the cosmetic, over the counter tier.** This is the strength you find in serums and creams sold without a prescription in most of Europe and online. Legally it is a cosmetic, not a drug, so it cannot make medical claims, but a well made 10% at the right pH delivers real benefit for maintenance and milder concerns. Many popular affordable serums sit here.

**15% gel is the prescription rosacea tier.** Sold as Finacea in the United States and as branded gels elsewhere, the 15% gel is the formulation studied and approved specifically for papulopustular rosacea. The gel base is engineered for penetration, which is part of why a 15% gel can perform like a higher percentage cream.

**20% cream is the prescription acne tier.** Sold as Azelex or Skinoren, the 20% cream is the strength with the longest trial history for acne vulgaris. It is the concentration behind most of the original efficacy data.

One point trips people up constantly: a 15% gel is not necessarily weaker than a 20% cream. The vehicle matters as much as the number. A gel with better follicular delivery can match or beat a higher percentage in a heavier cream base. This is why comparing azelaic acid products by percentage alone is misleading, and why our [ingredient encyclopedia](/encyclopedia) records the base and pH, not just the headline figure.

## Does a higher percentage work better?

Not automatically. Beyond a certain point, more azelaic acid does not mean proportionally more benefit, because tolerance, formulation and consistency decide the real world result.

The evidence is clearest in rosacea, where the 15% gel is the reference. A phase 3 randomised, double blind, vehicle controlled trial of 15% azelaic acid foam for papulopustular rosacea found significantly higher treatment success on investigator global assessment and a significantly greater fall in inflammatory lesion count versus vehicle, with side effects that stayed mild to moderate and local ([Draelos et al., 2015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26244354/)). In a head to head trial, 15% azelaic acid gel was superior to 0.75% metronidazole gel, reducing the mean inflammatory lesion count by 12.9 versus 10.7 over 15 weeks ([Elewski et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14623704/)). Nobody needed 20% to get that result.

For acne, the 20% cream carries the weight of history, but it is not the strongest option on the shelf overall. A randomised, assessor blinded trial comparing benzoyl peroxide 3% plus clindamycin 1% against azelaic acid 20% cream found azelaic acid delivered a solid median reduction in inflammatory lesions of 65.3% at week 12, while the benzoyl peroxide combination reached 78.8% ([Stein Gold et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26915831/)). Azelaic acid works well; it is simply a gentler, slower tool than a peroxide combination. That trade of raw speed for tolerability is the whole point of the ingredient.

The practical reading: for rosacea, 15% is the proven strength and 20% adds little. For acne, 20% is well studied but not a magic ceiling, and a consistent 10% often beats an abandoned 20%. The best strength is the one you keep using.

## Azelaic acid 10% vs 20% by skin concern

Match the strength to your dominant concern rather than defaulting to the highest number you can buy.

### Mild breakouts and maintenance

Start with 10%. For the odd breakout, an oily T zone or keeping cleared skin calm, a cosmetic 10% serum is enough and easier to use nightly without flaking. It slots neatly into a simple routine and pairs with niacinamide. If your acne is persistent and inflammatory, that is when a prescription strength or a different active earns consideration. Our [dermatologist approved acne routine](/blog/acne-routine-dermatologist-approved/) shows where azelaic acid fits among cleansers and other treatments.

### Moderate acne

Consider 20%, ideally with a doctor. Moderate, papular acne is the scenario the 20% cream was built and trialled for. That said, azelaic acid is often a support act here rather than the lead, because benzoyl peroxide and retinoids move faster on inflammatory acne. If you are weighing your options, our comparison of [salicylic acid versus benzoyl peroxide](/blog/salicylic-acid-vs-benzoyl-peroxide-acne/) explains where each active shines, and azelaic acid frequently works best alongside them, not instead of them.

### Rosacea

Go straight to 15% gel if you can. This is the one concern where the prescription strength has a clear evidence lead over the cosmetic tier, and where 15% gel beat both vehicle and metronidazole in controlled trials ([Elewski et al., 2003](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/14623704/)). A 10% cosmetic serum can help maintain calm skin between flares, but for active papulopustular rosacea the 15% gel is the target. Our [rosacea skincare routine guide](/blog/rosacea-skincare-routine-what-to-avoid/) covers the triggers and supporting steps that make any azelaic acid strength work harder.

### Dark spots and melasma

Either works, and 10% is a sensible start. Azelaic acid fades pigmentation by inhibiting tyrosinase, and the review evidence puts it at least on par with hydroquinone for melasma ([Fitton and Goa, 1991](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/1712709/)). For post inflammatory marks after acne, a 10% serum used consistently with daily sunscreen does the job for many people. Escalate to a prescription strength for stubborn, long standing melasma. To build a full brightening routine, see our roundup of the [best serums for hyperpigmentation](/blog/best-serums-hyperpigmentation-dark-spots/).

### Sensitive or reactive skin

Begin at 10% and go slow. Azelaic acid is already one of the gentlest actives, but a small number of people feel a transient tingle or itch in the first weeks at any strength. Starting low lets your skin adapt before you decide whether more is needed. Many people never need to move past 10%.

## How to use azelaic acid safely at any strength

Use the lowest strength that meets your goal, apply a pea sized amount, and give it 8 to 12 weeks before judging results.

A safe starting protocol looks like this:

1. **Pick your strength by concern, not by ambition.** 10% for maintenance, mild acne and early marks. 15% gel for rosacea. 20% cream for moderate acne, ideally prescribed.
2. **Start once daily in the evening.** Apply to clean, dry skin. If your skin is reactive, start every other night for the first two weeks.
3. **Use a thin, even layer.** A pea sized amount covers the whole face. More product does not mean faster results, it means more chance of tingling.
4. **Follow with a barrier moisturiser.** A cream with ceramides settles the mild flush some people feel early on. Our explainer on [ceramides and the skin barrier](/blog/ceramides-explained-why-your-barrier-needs-them/) covers why that helps.
5. **Wear sunscreen every morning.** This matters most for pigmentation, because unprotected sun undoes any tyrosinase inhibition you gain overnight.
6. **Give it real time.** Expect 8 to 12 weeks for a visible change in acne, redness or dark marks. Azelaic acid is a slow, steady worker, not an overnight fix.

For where azelaic acid sits relative to your other steps, our guide to [skincare routine order](/blog/skincare-routine-order-morning-night/) maps the full morning and evening sequence.

### What not to layer the same evening

Azelaic acid is friendly with most actives, but a few combinations raise the irritation risk without adding benefit:

- **A fresh retinoid start.** If you are new to both, introduce them on alternate nights rather than together, so you can tell which one your skin is reacting to.
- **Strong exfoliating acids.** Stacking azelaic acid with a glycolic or high strength salicylic acid the same night overloads the barrier. Alternate instead.
- **High strength vitamin C in the same layer.** Not dangerous, but the two can feel redundant and occasionally sting together. Split them between morning and evening.

The reassuring news is that azelaic acid at 10% pairs comfortably with niacinamide, hyaluronic acid and gentle moisturisers, which makes it one of the easiest actives to fit into an existing routine.

## Can you get 20% without a prescription?

In most of Europe and North America, no. The 15% gel and 20% cream are regulated as medicines, so they require a prescription, while 10% is sold freely as a cosmetic. This is the practical constraint that decides many people's choice: if you cannot easily see a doctor, a well formulated 10% is your realistic ceiling, and it is a genuinely useful one.

There is a grey area online where products labelled above 10% appear on international marketplaces. Treat those with caution. An unregulated high percentage in an unknown base is not a safer bet than a properly formulated 10% from a reputable brand. Judge the formula, not just the number, and check how a product scores on stability and base quality rather than the headline percentage. For how we assess that, see our [methodology](/methodology).

If your concern is rosacea or moderate acne that a 10% has not resolved after a full 12 week trial, that is the moment to ask a dermatologist about a prescription strength. Bringing a record of what you have already tried makes that conversation faster and more useful.

## Which strength should you buy?

Buy 10% if you are new to azelaic acid, want daily maintenance, have mild breakouts, or are treating early post acne marks. It is the low friction entry point and it works for the majority of everyday concerns. Move to a 15% gel if you have diagnosed papulopustular rosacea, because that is the strength with the strongest evidence for it. Reach for a 20% cream, ideally prescribed, if you have moderate acne that a lower strength or gentler routine has not settled.

Whatever you choose, remember that formulation beats percentage. A 10% at a good pH in a stable base can outperform a poorly made 20%, and consistency over three months beats any strength used for three weeks. ## Frequently asked questions

### Is azelaic acid 20% better than 10%?

Not always. For rosacea, the 15% gel has the strongest evidence, and 20% adds little over it. For moderate acne, the 20% cream is well studied and delivered a median 65.3% cut in inflammatory lesions at 12 weeks in one trial ([Stein Gold et al., 2016](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/26915831/)), but a consistent 10% often beats a stronger product used sporadically. Formulation quality and daily use matter more than the percentage.

### Can I buy azelaic acid 20% without a prescription?

In most countries, no. The 15% gel and 20% cream are regulated as medicines and need a prescription, while 10% is sold as a cosmetic over the counter. Unregulated high percentage products sold online in unknown bases are not a reliable substitute for a properly formulated 10% or a prescribed strength.

### How long does azelaic acid take to work at any strength?

Expect 8 to 12 weeks for a visible change, whether you use 10%, 15% or 20%. Acne and redness usually improve first; dark spots and melasma take the longest because pigment fades slowly. Increasing the strength does not shortcut this timeline, it only raises the chance of early irritation.

### Is azelaic acid safe during pregnancy?

Azelaic acid is widely regarded as one of the more pregnancy compatible actives for acne and rosacea, which is part of why dermatologists favour it when retinoids and some other options are off the table. Always confirm with your own doctor before starting any active while pregnant or breastfeeding. The peer reviewed resource DermNet summarises its uses and safety profile on its [azelaic acid page](https://dermnetnz.org/topics/azelaic-acid).

### Can I use azelaic acid with retinol or acids?

Yes, but not carelessly. Azelaic acid layers comfortably with niacinamide, hyaluronic acid and moisturisers. Avoid starting it on the same night as a fresh retinoid, and do not stack it with a strong exfoliating acid in one application. Alternate strong actives across the week and follow the sequence in our [skincare routine order guide](/blog/skincare-routine-order-morning-night/).

## The bottom line

Azelaic acid 10% and 20% are two points on the same gentle, three job ingredient, not rival technologies. The 10% cosmetic tier handles maintenance, mild acne and early pigmentation, and it is the right starting point for almost everyone. The 15% gel is the proven strength for rosacea, and the 20% cream is the studied strength for moderate acne, both prescription only in most places. The number on the tube matters far less than the quality of the formula and your willingness to use it for a full three months. Choose by your concern, start low, protect your barrier, wear sunscreen, and let the ingredient do its slow, reliable work.
