If you have spent any time reading Japanese skincare recommendations, you have met Hada Labo. The clear bottle with the spare blue label is one of the best selling skincare products in Japan, and its Gokujyun lotion has reportedly sold a bottle every few seconds in the domestic market for years. The brand built that position on one idea executed cheaply and well: multi weight hyaluronic acid in a no frills hydration step. This **Hada Labo guide** ranks the lines that matter, explains how the Japanese lotion step actually works, and shows where the formulas earn their reputation and where they fall short on [SkinScore](/rankings).

> **TL;DR**: Hada Labo is a Rohto Pharmaceutical brand built on multi weight hyaluronic acid. The core line, **Gokujyun**, is a budget hydration range, and the **Gokujyun Premium Lotion** with five forms of hyaluronic acid is the single product worth the hype. **Shirojyun** adds arbutin and vitamin C for brightness, the **Premium and Tokujyun** lines add ceramides and richer textures, and the anti aging line adds retinol. The "lotion" is a hydrating toner you press into damp skin in layers, not a Western milky lotion. Watch for added fragrance and alcohol in several formulas, which can irritate reactive skin. For pure hydration value, very little competes at the price.

## What Is Hada Labo and Why Japan Loves It

Hada Labo launched in 2004 under Rohto Pharmaceutical, a company that has sold medicines and skincare in Japan since 1899. The name translates roughly as "skin laboratory," and the positioning is deliberately clinical: short ingredient lists, plain packaging, and a single hero molecule rather than a long marketing story. The brand sits in the drugstore tier, the same shelf logic that makes [The Ordinary](/blog/the-ordinary-complete-guide-best-products/) so popular in the West, but with a J-beauty hydration philosophy instead of a single active per bottle.

The hero molecule is hyaluronic acid. Where many brands list one form, Hada Labo built its identity on layering several molecular weights of hyaluronic acid in one product so that water is held at different depths of the upper skin. The Japanese name for the flagship line, Gokujyun, means something close to "extreme moisture," and that is the whole pitch. The brand leaned into hydration as the foundation of a routine long before "skin barrier" became a marketing word in the West.

It matters that Rohto is a pharmaceutical company, not a pure cosmetics house. The formulas are competent, the preservation is sensible, and the price stays low because the spend goes into the formula rather than packaging or celebrity campaigns. That is the same value logic that rewards evidence first shopping everywhere, and it is why a plain Japanese hydration toner can outperform products that cost ten times as much on the metric that matters for dry, tight skin.

## The Hada Labo Lines Explained: Gokujyun, Shirojyun, Premium and More

The catalog looks confusing because the names are Japanese and several lines overlap. Here is the map.

**Gokujyun** is the core hydration line, identified by the clear bottle and blue text. It covers a cleansing foam, the famous lotion, an essence, a milk, a gel and a cream. Everything in it is organized around multi weight hyaluronic acid. This is where most people should start.

**Gokujyun Premium** is the upgraded version of the core line. The Premium Lotion is the product the brand is most famous for, formulated with five types of hyaluronic acid. It is thicker, more cushioning and the best single expression of what Hada Labo does.

**Shirojyun** is the brightening line, identified by white packaging. Shiro means white, and the line targets dullness and uneven tone with arbutin and a vitamin C derivative alongside the hyaluronic acid base. It is aimed at hyperpigmentation and overall radiance rather than pure hydration.

**Tokujyun** is a Southeast Asian and export line that sits between Gokujyun and Premium, often with hyaluronic acid plus collagen messaging. Availability varies by region, so you may not see it depending on where you shop.

**Anti aging (sometimes labeled with retinol or "perfect gel")** adds retinol and other firming actives on top of the hydration base, aimed at mature skin. Treat the retinol versions as actual retinoid products, with the same cautions any retinol carries.

**Mentholatum and pharmacy crossover products** exist because Rohto also owns Mentholatum, so you will occasionally see overlapping sunscreen or lip care in the same retail space. Those are separate ranges and not part of the Hada Labo hydration story.

The practical takeaway: pick one line based on your main goal. Gokujyun or Gokujyun Premium for hydration, Shirojyun for brightness, the anti aging line for firmness. Do not buy across all three at once.

## The Star Product: Gokujyun Premium Hyaluronic Acid Lotion

If you buy one Hada Labo product, buy the Gokujyun Premium Lotion. It is the reason the brand has the reputation it does.

The formula combines several molecular weights of hyaluronic acid so that hydration is held at different levels of the stratum corneum. High molecular weight hyaluronic acid forms a hydrating film on the surface, while lower molecular weight fractions sit deeper in the upper layers. This layered approach is the entire point of the product and the reason it feels more substantial than a single weight hyaluronic acid serum. We go deeper on why molecular weight matters, and where the marketing overreaches, in our [hyaluronic acid mistakes guide](/blog/hyaluronic-acid-mistakes-everyone-makes/).

The texture is the surprising part. Despite being called a lotion, it is a clear, slightly viscous liquid that turns tacky as you press it in, then settles into skin that feels plumped rather than greasy. It is fragrance free in the core Premium formulation in most markets, which makes it a safer pick than several other products in the range. It layers cleanly under serums, oils and sunscreen.

Where it earns its grade is the ratio of result to price. Hyaluronic acid is a humectant, which means it draws and holds water but does not repair the barrier or treat a concern on its own. Judged as what it is, a high quality, well preserved, multi weight humectant toner at a drugstore price, it is one of the strongest value products in skincare. Judged as a miracle anti aging serum, which is how some marketing frames it, it will disappoint. Buy it for hydration and your expectations will be met.

## How to Use a Japanese Lotion: The Layering Method

The single biggest source of confusion for Western users is the word "lotion." In Japanese skincare, a lotion (keshohin) is a watery hydrating toner applied after cleansing, not the milky moisturizer the word means in English. Misreading this is why some people apply Hada Labo wrong and conclude it does nothing.

The traditional method is layering. After cleansing, while the skin is still slightly damp, you dispense the lotion into your palms, warm it, and press it into the face in thin layers rather than wiping it on with a cotton pad. A common approach in Japan is to layer two or three light passes, letting each absorb for a few seconds, until the skin feels saturated and supple. Pressing with the palms, sometimes called the "patting" or chin pressing method, is meant to push the humectant into the upper skin and avoid the friction of a cotton pad.

Order matters. Apply the lotion on damp skin, because hyaluronic acid is a humectant and works best when there is water available to bind. On bone dry skin in a dry climate, a humectant can pull moisture from the deeper skin instead of the air, which is the opposite of what you want. The fix is simple: apply to damp skin and always seal with a moisturizer or oil on top to lock the water in. This sequencing fits inside the wider logic of our [skincare routine order guide](/blog/skincare-routine-order-morning-night/): cleanse, hydrating toner, treatments, then occlusive last.

A realistic Hada Labo step looks like this. Cleanse, then press two layers of Gokujyun Premium Lotion into damp skin, wait until it stops feeling tacky, apply any serum, then a moisturizer or cream to seal. That is the routine the brand was designed around.

## How SkinScore Rates Hada Labo

Every product on SkinScore is graded on its INCI list rather than its marketing, and Hada Labo is a useful case study because the brand's reputation is mostly deserved but not uniform across the range.

The hydration formulas score well on what they set out to do. Multi weight hyaluronic acid, glycerin and a sensible base earn a strong grade for a humectant step, and the core Premium Lotion benefits from being fragrance free in most markets. The scoring rewards exactly this kind of focused, well preserved formula that does one job competently.

The grade drops where the brand adds fragrance and alcohol. Several Hada Labo formulas, particularly some regional versions and the richer creams, contain added fragrance, and a few include denatured alcohol relatively high on the list to adjust texture and feel. On reactive, rosacea prone or eczema prone skin those are documented triggers, which is why a "calming" hydration product can still irritate. If you are sensitive, the formulation, not the brand name, decides whether a given bottle suits you, the same principle we apply in our [fragrance as a hidden allergen guide](/blog/fragrance-skincare-hidden-allergen/).

The practical upshot is that you should grade by line and by region, not by logo. The fragrance free Premium hydration products tend to score highest. The scented brightening and richer cream formulas score lower for sensitive skin specifically, even when they are perfectly fine for tolerant skin. ## Hada Labo by Skin Concern

Match the line to your actual goal rather than buying the most famous bottle by default.

**Dehydration, tightness and dry skin.** This is the brand's home turf. Gokujyun or Gokujyun Premium Lotion layered on damp skin, sealed with a richer cream, is a strong, cheap hydration foundation. For genuinely dry skin that needs barrier lipids rather than just water, pair it with a ceramide moisturizer, because hyaluronic acid alone does not replace lipids. Our [ceramides guide](/blog/ceramides-explained-why-your-barrier-needs-them/) explains why the barrier needs both.

**Dullness and uneven tone.** The Shirojyun brightening line, with arbutin and a vitamin C derivative, is the targeted option. Set expectations: drugstore brightening actives work slowly and modestly compared with a dedicated treatment, and they belong alongside daily sunscreen, not instead of it. For a fuller picture of how vitamin C forms differ in strength, see our [vitamin C serum guide](/blog/vitamin-c-serum-guide-forms-concentrations/).

**Oily and combination skin.** The lightweight Gokujyun lotion and gel formats suit oily skin that wants hydration without heaviness. Skip the richest creams and the milk if you break out easily, and apply thin layers. A humectant step is still useful for oily skin, because dehydrated oily skin often overproduces oil to compensate.

**Sensitive and reactive skin.** Favor the fragrance free Premium hydration products and avoid the scented brightening and richer lines. Patch test on the inner arm for three days before putting any new product on the face. If your skin reacts unpredictably, a humectant toner is one of the lower risk steps, but only when it is genuinely fragrance free.

**Mature skin.** The anti aging line adds retinol on top of the hydration base. Treat it as a retinoid product, introduce it slowly, and pair it with daily sun protection. Retinol is the most evidence backed topical for fine lines, and the [American Academy of Dermatology](https://www.aad.org/public/everyday-care/skin-care-secrets/anti-aging/retinoid-retinol) lists retinoids as a first line anti aging active. Note that retinoids are contraindicated in pregnancy, a point the [ANSM](https://ansm.sante.fr) and dermatology consensus are firm on.

## Building a Full Routine With Hada Labo

Hada Labo is a hydration specialist, not a complete skincare line. It has competent cleansers and excellent hydrating toners, but it is not where you should source your sunscreen or your only treatment active. Build around its strengths.

**Morning, normal to dry skin.**

1. Gentle cleanser or a water rinse
2. Two layers of Gokujyun Premium Lotion on damp skin
3. A serum if you use one, for example a vitamin C derivative
4. A ceramide moisturizer to seal
5. Broad spectrum SPF 30 to 50, ideally a separate dedicated sunscreen

**Evening, hydration focused.**

1. Cleanser
2. Two to three layers of Gokujyun Premium Lotion on damp skin
3. A treatment in its own slot, for example retinol on alternate nights
4. Moisturizer or a light occlusive to lock everything in

The principle is the one that runs through every good routine: light and water based first, treatments in the middle, occlusive last. The Hada Labo lotion is the water based hydration layer near the start, and it makes the rest of the routine work better by giving actives a hydrated surface to sit on. For the full sequencing logic across morning and night, follow our [routine order guide](/blog/skincare-routine-order-morning-night/).

One honest limitation: do not expect a hydrating toner to treat acne, rosacea or pigmentation by itself. It supports those routines by keeping the skin comfortable and reducing the irritation that derails active use, but the treatment work is done by the active in the treatment slot, not by the hyaluronic acid.

## Hada Labo Versus Western Drugstore Brands

The natural comparison is to Western drugstore hydration. Against a single weight hyaluronic acid serum from a budget Western brand, the Gokujyun Premium Lotion's multi weight blend and layering format give a more cushioned, more complete hydration step for a similar price. The J-beauty approach of pressing in light layers also tends to deliver a better finish than slapping on one heavy serum.

Against barrier focused Western brands built on ceramides, the comparison is different, because they are solving different problems. Hada Labo holds water; a ceramide cream replaces lipids. The smart move is to use both, a Hada Labo lotion for the humectant layer and a ceramide moisturizer to seal, rather than treating them as competitors. The same logic that makes us recommend pairing actives with the right base applies here, and it is why our [CeraVe versus Cetaphil comparison](/blog/cerave-vs-cetaphil-which-moisturizer-actually-better/) keeps coming back to formulation fit rather than brand loyalty.

Where Hada Labo clearly wins is price to performance on hydration specifically. Where it clearly loses is breadth: there is no standout sunscreen, no strong cleanser worth seeking out, and the fragrance in several formulas rules it out for the most reactive skin. Use it as a specialist, not a one brand routine.

## Common Mistakes With Hada Labo

A few avoidable errors explain most disappointed reviews.

**Applying it on dry skin in a dry climate.** A humectant on bone dry skin with dry air can pull water from the deeper skin. Apply on damp skin and always seal on top.

**Treating the lotion as a moisturizer.** It is a hydrating toner, not the final step. Without an occlusive to lock the water in, the hydration evaporates and the skin can feel tight again within an hour.

**Buying the scented line for sensitive skin.** Several formulas contain fragrance. If you are reactive, choose the fragrance free Premium hydration products and patch test first.

**Expecting anti aging miracles from hyaluronic acid.** It plumps and hydrates, which softens the look of fine lines temporarily, but it does not rebuild collagen. For that you need a retinoid or another evidence backed active in the treatment slot.

**Over layering.** Pressing in two or three light passes is the method. Drowning the face in product does not absorb better, it just sits on the surface and pills under sunscreen.

## FAQ

### What is the best Hada Labo product to buy first?

The best first purchase is the Gokujyun Premium Hyaluronic Acid Lotion. It is the flagship, built on five forms of hyaluronic acid, fragrance free in most markets, and the clearest expression of what the brand does well. Press two light layers into damp skin after cleansing, then seal with a moisturizer. It suits most skin types and is the best value entry point into the range.

### Is Hada Labo lotion a toner or a moisturizer?

It is a hydrating toner in the Japanese sense, not a Western milky moisturizer. The word "lotion" in J-beauty means a watery, humectant rich toner applied after cleansing and before serum and cream. It is not the final step. You still need a moisturizer or occlusive on top to lock the hydration in, otherwise the water it draws can evaporate.

### How do you use Hada Labo Gokujyun lotion?

Apply it on damp skin after cleansing. Dispense a small amount into your palms, warm it, and press it into the face in thin layers rather than wiping it on with a cotton pad. Layer two or three light passes, letting each absorb for a few seconds, until the skin feels supple. Follow with serum, then a moisturizer to seal. Layering and damp application are the keys to getting results.

### Is Hada Labo good for sensitive skin?

The fragrance free Premium hydration products can be good for sensitive skin, because hyaluronic acid is well tolerated and a humectant step is low risk. The catch is that several Hada Labo formulas, especially the richer creams and some brightening products, contain added fragrance and sometimes alcohol, which are common triggers on reactive skin. Choose the fragrance free options, check the current INCI list, and patch test for three days before using on the face.

### Does Hada Labo contain alcohol or fragrance?

Some formulas do. Added fragrance appears in several products in the range, and a few formulas use denatured alcohol relatively high on the list to adjust texture. The core Gokujyun Premium Lotion is fragrance free in most markets, which is part of why it scores well. Because the brand reformulates between regions, always read the actual INCI list of the specific product and market rather than assuming the whole brand is one way.

### Is Hada Labo better than The Ordinary?

They solve different problems, so neither is universally better. Hada Labo specializes in layered, multi weight hyaluronic acid hydration with a J-beauty application method, while The Ordinary offers a broad catalog of single ingredient actives. For a focused, cushioned hydration step, Hada Labo's Premium Lotion is excellent. For targeted treatment actives like retinol, niacinamide or acids, The Ordinary's range is wider. Many people use a Hada Labo lotion for hydration and The Ordinary for actives in the same routine.

### Can Hada Labo replace my moisturizer?

No. The lotion is a humectant step that draws and holds water, but it does not provide the barrier lipids or occlusion that prevent that water from evaporating. On its own, especially in dry air, it can leave the skin feeling tight again within the hour. Use it as the hydrating layer and seal it with a moisturizer or cream, ideally one with ceramides, to keep the hydration locked in.

## Bottom Line

Hada Labo deserves its reputation for one specific thing: cheap, well formulated, multi weight hyaluronic acid hydration, with the Gokujyun Premium Lotion as the standout. Understand that the "lotion" is a Japanese hydrating toner, apply it in light layers on damp skin, and always seal it with a moisturizer, and it becomes one of the best value steps you can add to a routine. Match the line to your goal rather than buying the most famous bottle, watch for fragrance if your skin is reactive, and treat it as a hydration specialist inside a wider routine rather than a complete skincare line. Check any product's current grade on [SkinScore](/rankings) before you buy, because formulas change between markets and the INCI list never does.
