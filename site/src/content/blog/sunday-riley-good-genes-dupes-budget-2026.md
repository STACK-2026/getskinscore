**Sunday Riley Good Genes dupes** exist because the product's active is not exclusive. Good Genes is a lactic acid treatment, and lactic acid is one of the most commoditized exfoliating acids in skincare. The results people credit to the cult bottle come from a well-understood alpha hydroxy acid that costs very little to formulate. This guide grades six budget alternatives by their actives, concentrations and evidence, not by their marketing.

> **TL;DR**: Good Genes runs around 85 dollars for 30ml and delivers a smoother, brighter finish from purified lactic acid. The same acid is available for a fraction of the price. The closest match on concentration is **The Ordinary Lactic Acid 5 percent + HA**, the strongest budget option is **The Ordinary Lactic Acid 10 percent + HA**, and both cost under ten dollars. **The Inkey List Lactic Acid** and **Good Molecules Overnight Exfoliating Treatment** are gentle beginner picks. **Paula's Choice 6 percent mandelic + 2 percent lactic** is the sensitive and pigment-prone pick. For the glycolic version of Good Genes, **The Ordinary Glycolic Acid 7 percent** is the answer. You are paying Sunday Riley for feel and packaging, not for a superior molecule.

## What Good Genes Actually Is

Good Genes is Sunday Riley's flagship exfoliating treatment. The original and most popular version is built around lactic acid, and the brand markets it as an all-in-one treatment that resurfaces, brightens and smooths the look of fine lines. It sits firmly in the luxury tier, priced at around 85 dollars for a 30ml bottle and about 122 dollars for the 50ml size on the [official Sunday Riley product page](https://www.sundayriley.com/products/good-genes-lactic-acid-treatment).

The formula pairs purified grade lactic acid with a supporting cast of extracts, including licorice, lemongrass and prickly pear, which the brand positions as soothing and brightening. Those extras are pleasant, but they are not what drives the exfoliation. The lactic acid does that job, and lactic acid behaves the same way whether it sits in an 85 dollar bottle or an 8 dollar one.

There is a second version worth flagging. Sunday Riley also sells a Good Genes Glycolic Acid Treatment, which swaps lactic acid for glycolic acid. If you have been using that one, your dupe target is different, and I cover it at the end of this guide.

The honest summary is that Good Genes is a competent, well-textured lactic acid serum with a premium price. Nothing about the active justifies the markup on results alone. What you pay for is the sensory experience, the packaging and the brand.

## Why Lactic Acid Is So Easy to Dupe

Lactic acid is an alpha hydroxy acid, or AHA. It works by loosening the bonds between dead surface skin cells so they shed more evenly, which reveals smoother, brighter skin underneath. It is also mildly humectant, meaning it holds a little water in the skin, so it tends to feel less stripping than glycolic acid at the same strength.

The key point for anyone hunting a dupe is that lactic acid is a single, defined molecule. It is not a proprietary blend or a patented delivery system. When a brand sells a lactic acid serum, the acid inside is chemically identical to the acid in every other lactic acid serum. The differences come down to concentration, pH, supporting ingredients and formulation feel, not to the acid itself.

The clinical case for lactic acid is solid. In a double-blind, vehicle-controlled trial published in the Archives of Dermatology, an 8 percent lactic acid cream produced measurable improvements in photodamaged skin over the study period, including firmer texture and reduced roughness, compared with the vehicle alone. You can read the record on [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8651713/). That study used a higher concentration than most leave-on serums, but it establishes that topical lactic acid does real work on skin quality.

One safety note that applies to every product in this guide. AHAs increase your skin's sensitivity to the sun during use and for a period after you stop, which is why daytime sunscreen is not optional when you exfoliate with acids. Paula's Choice covers this well in its [lactic acid guide](https://www.paulaschoice.com/skin-care-advice/exfoliants/lactic-acid-for-skin), and it is the single most important habit to pair with any of these serums. If you want the full breakdown of how AHAs compare with BHAs, see our [salicylic acid vs glycolic acid guide](/blog/salicylic-acid-vs-glycolic-acid-bha-aha-2026/).

## How I Ranked These Dupes

I judged each alternative on four things. First, the type and concentration of acid, because that determines how close it gets to the Good Genes experience. Second, the supporting formula, since humectants and soothing agents change tolerability. Third, the price per bottle, which is the whole reason you are here. Fourth, who the product actually suits, because the strongest option is not the best option for everyone.

I did not rank on scent, packaging or how photogenic the bottle is. Those are the exact things you overpay for with the original, so rewarding them here would defeat the purpose.

## The 6 Best Good Genes Dupes

### 1. The Ordinary Lactic Acid 5% + HA: the closest match

If you want the product that behaves most like the original Good Genes, this is it. The 5 percent version from The Ordinary sits close to the effective strength range of the original lactic acid treatment, and it adds hyaluronic acid for a little cushioning. It costs under ten dollars, which you can confirm on the [official product page](https://theordinary.com/en-us/lactic-acid-5-ha-serum-100434.html).

The texture is a light, slightly viscous serum. It exfoliates gently enough for most skin types to use several times a week once tolerance is built. For anyone who liked Good Genes but hesitated at the price, this is the first thing to try. You lose the fancy extracts and the plush feel, but you keep the result.

Best for: people who want a direct, gentle equivalent to the original.

### 2. The Ordinary Lactic Acid 10% + HA: the strongest budget option

This is the same line at double the concentration. At 10 percent lactic acid, it delivers a more noticeable resurfacing effect, so it suits people whose skin has adjusted to acids and who want more visible smoothing and brightening. It also includes hyaluronic acid and a small amount of Tasmanian pepperberry, which the brand includes to reduce the sting that stronger acids can cause. Check the current price on the [official product page](https://theordinary.com/en-us/lactic-acid-10-ha-exfoliator-100437.html).

Because it is stronger, it is also easier to overdo. Start with one or two nights a week and build up. If your skin feels tight, flaky or reactive, drop back to the 5 percent version. This is the pick for experienced acid users who want maximum effect for minimum spend.

Best for: seasoned users who want a stronger result than the original.

### 3. The Inkey List Lactic Acid Serum: the beginner pick

The Inkey List built its reputation on plain language and beginner-friendly formulas, and its lactic acid serum fits that brief. It uses lactic acid alongside hyaluronic acid and squalane, which makes it feel more moisturizing and forgiving than a bare acid serum. It usually lands a few dollars above the cheapest options, and you can see it on the [official product page](https://theinkeylist.com/products/lactic-acid).

The addition of squalane is the differentiator here. It softens the experience and helps offset the mild dryness that acids can cause, which is exactly what a nervous first-time exfoliator wants. If you found Good Genes appealing but you are new to acids, this is a low-risk place to start. For help slotting it into your routine, see our [morning and night routine order guide](/blog/skincare-routine-order-morning-night/).

Best for: first-time acid users who want a gentle, cushioned formula.

### 4. Good Molecules Overnight Exfoliating Treatment: the value champion

Good Molecules made its name on doing one thing per product at a rock-bottom price, and this overnight treatment is a strong lactic acid option for around fourteen dollars. It is designed as a leave-on nighttime step that resurfaces while you sleep, which is essentially how many people use Good Genes anyway. The current price and full ingredient list are on the [official product page](https://goodmolecules.com/products/overnight-exfoliating-treatment).

The formula is straightforward and lightweight, with no heavy fragrance to worry about. It will not feel as luxurious as the original, but it does the exfoliating work reliably. This is the pick if your only goal is results per dollar and you do not care about a premium finish.

Best for: strict budget shoppers who want a no-nonsense overnight acid.

### 5. Paula's Choice 6% Mandelic + 2% Lactic Acid: the sensitive and pigment pick

This one is not a pure lactic acid serum, and that is the point. Paula's Choice pairs 2 percent lactic acid with 6 percent mandelic acid, which is the largest common AHA molecule and therefore one of the gentlest and slowest to penetrate. The combination is well suited to sensitive skin and to people dealing with uneven tone and dark marks. You can review it on the [official product page](https://www.paulaschoice.com/skin-perfecting-6pct-mandelic-acid-and-2pct-lactic-acid-liquid-exfoliant/1470.html).

Mandelic acid has a reputation for being kinder to reactive and deeper skin tones while still helping with pigmentation, so this formula is a smart trade if your main concern is brightening without irritation. It costs more than The Ordinary options but far less than Good Genes. If dark spots are your priority, pair it with the advice in our [best serums for hyperpigmentation guide](/blog/best-serums-hyperpigmentation-dark-spots/).

Best for: sensitive, reactive or pigment-prone skin that wants gentle brightening.

### 6. The Ordinary Glycolic Acid 7%: for the glycolic Good Genes

If you have been using the Good Genes Glycolic Acid Treatment rather than the lactic version, your dupe is a glycolic product, not a lactic one. The Ordinary Glycolic Acid 7 percent Exfoliating Toner is the obvious budget answer. It is a leave-on toner rather than a serum, so it spreads thinly across the whole face, and it costs under ten dollars on the [official product page](https://theordinary.com/en-us/glycolic-acid-7-exfoliating-toner-100418.html).

Glycolic acid is a smaller molecule than lactic acid, so it penetrates faster and can feel stronger at the same percentage. Use it once every couple of nights to start, and never layer it with other strong acids on the same evening. This is the correct match only if the glycolic version of Good Genes is what you were replacing.

Best for: people replacing the glycolic Good Genes specifically.

## Good Genes vs the Dupes: Where the Money Goes

Line the products up and the pattern is obvious. Good Genes costs roughly 85 dollars. The Ordinary options cost under ten. Even the priciest pick here, the Paula's Choice dual-acid exfoliant, comes in at less than half the original. The acid doing the work is the same class of molecule in every case.

So what does the extra money buy with Sunday Riley? A more refined texture, a curated blend of botanical extracts, a heavier and more attractive bottle, and the brand experience. Those are real things, and some people value them enough to pay for them. That is a legitimate choice. What is not legitimate is the belief that the original works because of a secret ingredient. It does not. It works because lactic acid works.

## How to Read a Lactic Acid Label Before You Buy

Whether you buy the original or a dupe, a few label habits protect you from overpaying and over-exfoliating.

Look for the concentration. If a brand hides the percentage, you cannot judge strength or compare products. The Ordinary and Good Molecules state it plainly, which is one reason they are easy to recommend.

Check the position of the acid in the ingredient list. An acid listed near the top is present at a meaningful level. An acid buried near the bottom, below a long list of extracts, is likely there for marketing rather than effect.

Watch for a stack of other actives. If a serum combines a strong acid with retinol, vitamin C or a second acid, tolerability drops and irritation risk climbs. Simpler is safer when you are exfoliating. If you want to get fluent at decoding these lists, read our [guide to reading an INCI list](/blog/how-to-read-inci-list-skincare/).

Finally, ignore the word natural on the front of the bottle. It tells you nothing about whether the formula is effective or gentle. The concentration and the full ingredient list do.

## How to Use Any of These Without Wrecking Your Barrier

Start slow. Two nights a week is plenty for the first two or three weeks, regardless of which product you pick. Your skin needs time to adjust, and rushing is the fastest route to redness and flaking.

Apply at night. Acids are best used in the evening, partly because they raise sun sensitivity and partly because your barrier recovers overnight. Always follow with a moisturizer to keep the skin comfortable.

Wear sunscreen every morning. This is non-negotiable with any AHA. The sun sensitivity from lactic acid lingers even after you pause the product, so daily broad-spectrum protection is the habit that makes exfoliation safe and worthwhile.

Do not layer acids. Pick one exfoliating product and use it consistently. Combining Good Genes or a dupe with another acid, a scrub or a retinoid on the same night is how people end up with a damaged barrier and blame the acid rather than the overuse.

Listen to your skin. Mild tingling on application is normal. Stinging that lasts, persistent redness, tightness or flaking means you are doing too much. Cut back the frequency until your skin settles.

## The Verdict

Good Genes is a good product with a bad price-to-value ratio. The lactic acid inside is not special, and six cheaper serums deliver the same core benefit. For the closest gentle match, buy The Ordinary Lactic Acid 5 percent. For a stronger effect on experienced skin, buy the 10 percent. For beginners, The Inkey List or Good Molecules. For sensitive or pigment-prone skin, the Paula's Choice dual acid. For the glycolic version, The Ordinary Glycolic Acid 7 percent.

Buy the acid, not the badge, and put the difference toward a good sunscreen. That combination will do more for your skin than the luxury bottle ever could.

## Frequently asked questions

### What is the best Sunday Riley Good Genes dupe?

The closest match to the original lactic acid Good Genes is The Ordinary Lactic Acid 5 percent + HA, which sits near the same effective strength and costs under ten dollars. For a stronger result on skin that already tolerates acids, The Ordinary Lactic Acid 10 percent + HA is the better pick. Both use the same lactic acid as the original.

### Why is Good Genes so expensive if the active is common?

You are paying for texture, botanical extracts, packaging and brand experience, not for a rare ingredient. Lactic acid is a standard, non-exclusive alpha hydroxy acid that costs very little to formulate, which is why serums with the same acid sell for under ten dollars.

### Does a cheaper lactic acid serum actually work as well?

For the exfoliating and brightening result, yes. Lactic acid is a single defined molecule, so a 5 or 10 percent lactic acid from a budget brand performs the same core job as the acid in Good Genes. The differences are in feel, supporting ingredients and price, not in the acid itself.

### Is Good Genes lactic acid or glycolic acid?

The original and best known version is lactic acid. Sunday Riley also sells a separate Good Genes Glycolic Acid Treatment. If you used the glycolic one, your dupe should also be glycolic, such as The Ordinary Glycolic Acid 7 percent, rather than a lactic acid serum.

### How often should I use a lactic acid dupe?

Start with two nights a week and build up as your skin adjusts, using it in the evening and always following with moisturizer. Wear broad-spectrum sunscreen every morning, because AHAs raise sun sensitivity during use and for a period after you stop.
