**Hyaluronic acid molecular weight changes what the molecule actually does on your skin, not whether it works.** High molecular weight hyaluronic acid (HMW) stays on the surface and holds water like a sponge. Low molecular weight (LMW) slips between dead corneocytes and plumps superficial layers. Oligo hyaluronic acid is even smaller and acts close to the surface. None of these forms reach the living dermis from a topical, which is normal and not a failure. The best serums layer several sizes so you get a film, a plumping effect, and a smooth finish.

> **TL;DR**: **Hyaluronic acid molecular weight** is the single variable most people ignore when they buy a hyaluronic acid serum, and it explains why two products with the same percentage feel completely different. HMW hyaluronic acid (over 1000 kDa) forms a hydrating film on top of the stratum corneum and is excellent for tight, dry days. LMW hyaluronic acid (around 50 to 300 kDa) penetrates the upper layers of the stratum corneum and gives a softer, plumper finish. Oligo hyaluronic acid (under 10 kDa) is tiny and stays near the surface, with some anti inflammatory claims that are still being studied. Multi weight serums combine all three and tend to feel the best on most skin types. Sodium hyaluronate, the salt form, behaves the same way at the same weight. Always seal the serum with a moisturiser, especially in dry weather, or the water it pulls in can evaporate back out.

If you have ever wondered why a five euro hyaluronic acid serum and a fifty euro one list the same active, the answer is almost always molecular weight. The acid is chemically identical, but its size dictates how it sits on skin, how it feels, and what it does. This guide walks through the three main weight classes, how to spot them on a label, and how to actually use them so the hydration sticks around.

## What is hyaluronic acid and why does its size matter

Hyaluronic acid is a glycosaminoglycan, a long chain sugar molecule your body produces naturally in skin, joints, and eyes. In skin, it acts like a water magnet, and a single gram can bind up to 1000 times its weight in water. The American Academy of Dermatology classes it among the [humectants worth knowing in any basic moisturiser](https://www.aad.org/public/everyday-care/skin-care-basics/dry/dermatologists-tips-relieve-dry-skin), alongside glycerin and urea.

The molecule is built from repeating sugar units, and the length of that chain determines its molecular weight, usually measured in kilodaltons (kDa). A long chain sits higher in the skin and holds more water on the surface. A short chain sits a bit deeper in the stratum corneum and behaves like a humectant inside the upper layers. Same molecule, different lengths, different effects. That is the whole point of the molecular weight conversation. ## The three molecular weight classes you will actually see

Hyaluronic acid in cosmetics is grouped into three rough size categories. The cut offs vary slightly between suppliers but the practical buckets are stable.

High molecular weight (HMW) is anything above roughly 1000 kDa, with premium grades up to 1500 to 2000 kDa. It is the classic film former, with a slight tightening feel as the gel dries.

Low molecular weight (LMW) usually falls between 50 and 300 kDa. Shorter chains slip between corneocytes in the upper stratum corneum and act as humectants inside that layer, with a plumper, less filmy finish.

Oligo hyaluronic acid, often labelled oligo HA, sits below 10 kDa. The fragments stay near the surface and some studies suggest mild anti inflammatory and signalling effects, though the topical evidence is thinner than for HMW or LMW. A review of [hyaluronic acid in dermatology indexed on PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22735503/) summarises how fragment size shifts the biological response, especially around inflammation. The trap is assuming smaller is better. It is not, just different.

## How HMW hyaluronic acid works on skin

HMW is the workhorse of every drugstore hyaluronic acid serum: cheap, stable, and immediately visible. When the gel dries, it leaves a thin polymer film on the surface that holds water at the skin/air interface. You get a glassy finish and a measurable jump in surface hydration that lasts as long as the film stays intact.

The trade off is that HMW does not really penetrate. The chains are too long to slip between corneocytes, which is fine because that is not its job. Its job is to keep the surface humid so the barrier does not lose water as fast.

HMW shines on dry, tight, or post cleansing skin, but it has one weakness. In a low humidity room or on a cold winter walk, the film can dry out and the water it pulled in can evaporate back into the air, leaving skin tighter than before. The fix is to seal the serum with a moisturiser containing lipids, especially ceramides. Our guide to [ceramides and why your barrier needs them](/blog/ceramides-explained-why-your-barrier-needs-them/) covers how this works.

## How LMW hyaluronic acid works on skin

LMW chains, in the 50 to 300 kDa range, are short enough to slip between the loose corneocytes of the upper stratum corneum without forming a strong surface film. They act as humectants inside the outer layer, drawing water into the dead cells and giving them a fuller, more cushioned look.

Where HMW leaves a glassy stretch, LMW leaves a softer plumping that does not feel sticky and layers well under sunscreen and makeup. LMW is also more efficient inside richer creams, since it does not need to dry into a film to work. If you mostly use moisturisers rather than separate serums, LMW is probably already doing most of the work in your current product. ## Oligo hyaluronic acid: the smallest fragments and the controversy

Oligo HA, also called ultra low molecular weight HA, is the marketing star of recent years. Brands claim it penetrates deeply and triggers regeneration in the dermis. Here the science gets thinner and the marketing thicker.

The honest summary: oligo HA stays in the upper stratum corneum like other small humectants. It does not cross into the living dermis from a leave on serum, and any brand suggesting otherwise is overselling. It has modest evidence for mild signalling and anti inflammatory effects near the surface, which can support a soothing claim. A clinical review of [topical hyaluronic acid penetration on PubMed Central](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC6648810/) walks through how fragment size and vehicle change what reaches what depth.

Inserm, the French national medical research agency, notes in its [public files on skin biology](https://www.inserm.fr/dossier/peau/) that the stratum corneum is the major barrier to topical penetration. That is fine for hydration, which is a surface and upper layer phenomenon, but a problem if you bought oligo HA expecting a dermal filler in a bottle.

## Multi weight or cascading hyaluronic acid: does it actually do more

Premium hyaluronic acid serums almost always combine several molecular weights in one formula, marketed as multi weight, cascade, or fractional HA. The reasoning is sound. HMW gives the surface film and immediate plump. LMW gives upper layer cushioning. Oligo HA adds the small fragment effect. Together they cover more ground than any single size.

In practice, multi weight serums feel better and perform better in instrumental hydration studies than single weight serums at the same total percentage. Whether that justifies a five times price difference is a personal call, but the technical claim is real. The European Academy of Dermatology and Venereology has published [educational materials on hyaluronic acid in dermatology](https://eadv.org/) that touch on how layered formulations dominate the premium serum category.

## Sodium hyaluronate vs hyaluronic acid on a label

Both names appear on labels and behave the same way in skin at the same molecular weight. Sodium hyaluronate is the sodium salt of HA, more stable in water and easier to formulate, and it is what most modern serums actually contain even when marketing says "hyaluronic acid."

Some labels list variants like hydrolyzed sodium hyaluronate (lower MW, broken into shorter chains) and sodium hyaluronate crosspolymer (cross linked, swells and traps water like a microscopic sponge). Several of these on one INCI list signals a multi weight blend.

## How to spot molecular weight on an INCI list

INCI lists are not required to disclose molecular weight, so you rarely see numbers on the box. Two signals still help.

First, multiple HA names usually means a multi weight blend. Sodium hyaluronate plus hydrolyzed sodium hyaluronate plus sodium hyaluronate crosspolymer is a deliberate stack.

Second, marketing terms like "multi weight," "cascading," "fractional," or "low to high molecular weight" usually map to a real blend. Our [how to read INCI list skincare guide](/blog/how-to-read-inci-list-skincare/) covers the broader skill.

ANSM, the French cosmetics safety agency, runs a [public cosmetics surveillance system](https://ansm.sante.fr/) where adverse reactions get logged. Hyaluronic acid does not appear as a meaningful issue, which is why almost every dermatologist will green light it.

## Concentrations, expectations, and skin type

Cosmetic HA is usually formulated between 0.1 and 2 percent total. Above 2 percent the formula gets sticky without much added benefit, and many high end serums sit closer to 1 percent with a smart size mix.

For dry or mature skin, multi weight serums or a layered approach (HMW serum then rich moisturiser) feel most comfortable. Apply to slightly damp skin and lock in with a lipid rich cream.

For oily or combination skin, a lighter LMW serum under a gel cream usually wins, since heavy HMW films can feel suffocating with active sebum. Same logic if you live somewhere humid.

For sensitive skin, HA is one of the lowest risk hydrators available, but watch the rest of the formula. Fragrance, drying alcohol, and irritating preservatives will still react even if the HA itself is gentle.

## Common mistakes when using hyaluronic acid serums

The biggest mistake is applying HA to bone dry skin in a dry room and skipping moisturiser. In low humidity the humectant has no water in the air to pull from, so it pulls from your skin and accelerates dehydration. Apply to slightly damp skin and always seal with a cream.

The second mistake is layering HA with strong actives without a buffer. HA on a fresh glycolic acid toner will sting more than the same serum applied ten minutes later. Our [vitamin C serum guide on forms and concentrations](/blog/vitamin-c-serum-guide-forms-concentrations/) shows how to pair active serums in the right order.

The third mistake is chasing percentages. A 2 percent single weight HMW serum is not better than a 1 percent multi weight blend. Format and size mix matter more than the headline number.

## Frequently asked questions

**Is high or low molecular weight hyaluronic acid better for skin?**
Neither is better in absolute terms, they do different jobs. High molecular weight HA forms a hydrating film on the surface and gives an immediate plumping feel, while low molecular weight HA slips between corneocytes and hydrates the upper layers. The most effective serums combine both sizes plus oligo HA so you get surface film, deeper plumping, and a soft finish in one product.

**Does low molecular weight hyaluronic acid penetrate the dermis?**
No, and any marketing that says otherwise is exaggerating. Low molecular weight HA penetrates the upper stratum corneum but does not reach the living dermis from a topical leave on product. The stratum corneum is designed to block almost everything, including small humectants. Surface and upper layer hydration is still what visibly transforms tight, dull skin.

**What is oligo hyaluronic acid?**
Oligo hyaluronic acid is the smallest commercial form, usually under 10 kDa, made by cutting the HA chain into very short fragments. It stays near the surface like other small humectants and has some published evidence for mild anti inflammatory and signalling effects, but it is not a dermal filler in a bottle. It is most useful as one piece of a multi weight blend, not as the only HA in a serum.

**Is sodium hyaluronate the same as hyaluronic acid?**
Sodium hyaluronate is the sodium salt of hyaluronic acid and behaves the same way on skin at the same molecular weight. The salt form is more stable and easier to formulate, so it is what most modern serums actually contain, even when the marketing says hyaluronic acid. You do not need to choose between them on a label.

**Can I use a hyaluronic acid serum every day?**
Yes, hyaluronic acid is one of the gentlest skincare ingredients available and is safe for daily morning and evening use on almost all skin types. The only practical rule is to apply it to slightly damp skin and to follow with a moisturiser, especially in dry or low humidity environments, so the water it pulls in does not evaporate back out.

**Does hyaluronic acid replace a moisturiser?**
No, a hyaluronic acid serum is a humectant treatment, not a complete moisturiser. It pulls and holds water but does not provide the lipids your skin needs to seal that water in. You still need a cream or lotion on top to prevent transepidermal water loss, particularly if your skin is dry, mature, or compromised.

## Bottom line

Hyaluronic acid molecular weight is the variable that explains almost every difference between two HA serums, and it is the variable almost no brand puts on the box. High molecular weight gives you a surface film and immediate plump. Low molecular weight slips into the upper layers and gives a softer, more cushioned finish. Oligo hyaluronic acid is small and useful as part of a blend but not a magic dermal active. Multi weight serums combine all three and tend to outperform single size formulas at the same percentage. Use any of them on slightly damp skin, follow with a moisturiser, and you will get the result the molecule is actually capable of, regardless of the price on the box.
