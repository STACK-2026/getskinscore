**Salicylic acid vs glycolic acid** comes down to one chemistry fact: salicylic acid is oil soluble and dives into the pore, while glycolic acid is water soluble and works across the skin surface. That single difference decides which one belongs in your routine. Salicylic acid (a BHA) is the better choice for oily, congested and acne prone skin. Glycolic acid (an AHA) is the better choice for dull, rough, sun damaged or uneven toned skin. Most people only need one. Some skin types benefit from both, used on different days.

> **TL;DR**: Pick **salicylic acid (BHA)** for blackheads, clogged pores and oily skin, because it is oil soluble and exfoliates inside the follicle. Pick **glycolic acid (AHA)** for texture, dullness, fine lines and dark patches, because it is water soluble and resurfaces the top of the skin. Sensitive or dry skin does better with **lactic acid** or low percentage salicylic acid than with glycolic acid. Never layer either one with retinol the same evening, and always finish your morning routine with SPF 30 or higher.

Chemical exfoliants replaced scrubs in the dermatologist toolkit for a good reason: they dissolve the bonds holding dead cells together instead of grinding skin with abrasive particles. The two most common are salicylic acid, a beta hydroxy acid, and glycolic acid, an alpha hydroxy acid. They sound interchangeable. They are not. This guide walks through both with peer reviewed evidence, then shows you exactly which to choose and how to use it without wrecking your barrier. ## The quick answer, in one table

Salicylic acid and glycolic acid solve different problems. Use this table to find your match before reading the detail.

| Feature | Salicylic acid (BHA) | Glycolic acid (AHA) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Acid class | Beta hydroxy acid | Alpha hydroxy acid |
| Solubility | Oil soluble | Water soluble |
| Where it works | Inside the pore and follicle | On the skin surface |
| Best for | Oily, acne prone, congested, blackheads | Dull, rough, sun damaged, uneven tone, fine lines |
| Main action | Keratolytic, unclogs pores, anti inflammatory | Loosens surface dead cells, brightens, smooths |
| Typical OTC strength | 0.5% to 2% | 5% to 10% leave on, up to 30% professional peel |
| Onset | 2 to 4 weeks | 2 to 6 weeks |
| Main side effect | Dryness, mild flaking | Stinging, redness, sun sensitivity |
| Pregnancy use | Topical OK below 2%, rinse off preferred | Generally considered low risk topically, ask a doctor |
| Pairs well with | Niacinamide, ceramides, hyaluronic acid | Niacinamide, ceramides, hyaluronic acid |
| Avoid same evening as | Retinol, AHAs, vitamin C (L-AA) | Retinol, BHAs, vitamin C (L-AA) |

If you remember one line: salicylic acid cleans out the pore, glycolic acid resurfaces the top. The American Academy of Dermatology lists both as evidence backed options within its [acne treatment guidance](https://www.aad.org/public/diseases/acne/derm-treat/treat).

## What salicylic acid actually does

Salicylic acid is the better exfoliant for anyone whose main problem is clogged pores, oiliness or breakouts, because it is the only common exfoliating acid that is oil soluble.

That word, oil soluble, is the whole story. The lining of a pore and the sebum that plugs it are oily. A water soluble acid sits on the surface and cannot reach inside. Salicylic acid, a lipophilic beta hydroxy acid derived from willow bark, dissolves into the sebum and works on the follicle wall from the inside. It loosens the desmosomes, the protein rivets holding dead keratinocytes together, so the plug breaks up and the pore drains. Dermatologists call this action keratolytic.

It does more than unclog. Salicylic acid is structurally related to aspirin, and it carries mild anti inflammatory activity that calms the redness around active spots. In a 2021 clinical study of 45 patients with mild to moderate acne, a salicylic acid based peel and gel protocol improved acne severity scores across the whole group ([Calvisi, 2021](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/34318988/)). A separate comparative trial found that salicylic acid peels were especially effective on non inflammatory, comedonal lesions, the blackheads and whiteheads that respond poorly to surface only treatments ([Dayal et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31553119/)).

Who should reach for salicylic acid first:

- Oily skin that looks shiny by midday
- Blackheads on the nose, chin and forehead
- Closed comedones, the small flesh coloured bumps that never come to a head
- Mild to moderate acne, used alongside a routine, not as the only step
- Body breakouts, where a salicylic acid wash reaches the back and chest

## What glycolic acid actually does

Glycolic acid is the better exfoliant for anyone whose main concern is dull, rough or uneven skin, because it resurfaces the outermost layer faster and more completely than any other acid in common use.

Glycolic acid is an alpha hydroxy acid made from sugar cane. It has the smallest molecule of all the AHAs, which is why it penetrates the stratum corneum quickly and acts strongly. It is water soluble, so it works on the surface, where it weakens the bonds between dead surface cells and triggers them to shed. The result is a smoother, more reflective skin surface and, over weeks, a measurable increase in epidermal turnover. The principle was shown decades ago for the AHA family: lactic acid stimulated epidermal turnover in a way that depended on both pH and concentration ([Smith, 1998](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/9648571/)), and the same surface resurfacing mechanism underlies modern glycolic acid use.

Because it works at the surface and increases cell renewal, glycolic acid also addresses signs of photoaging. Hydroxy acids have a documented role in improving the texture and appearance of sun damaged skin ([Edison et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15002656/)), and in clinical practice glycolic acid peels are combined with other agents to target uneven tone and rough texture ([Abdel Meguid et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31603267/)). Over time, regular glycolic acid use can soften early fine lines, fade post acne marks and brighten a tired complexion.

Who should reach for glycolic acid first:

- Normal to dry skin that looks dull or flat
- Rough texture you can feel under your fingertips
- Early fine lines and sun damage
- Uneven tone and lingering dark marks (use with sunscreen)
- Flaky patches that makeup clings to

A word of caution: glycolic acid raises sun sensitivity more noticeably than salicylic acid, so daytime SPF is not optional. ## Salicylic acid vs glycolic acid by skin concern

The fastest way to choose is to match the acid to your dominant concern rather than to a brand or a trend.

### Oily skin and large pores

Salicylic acid wins. Its oil solubility lets it work inside an oily pore where glycolic acid cannot reach. It also reduces the look of enlarged pores over time by keeping them clear. If you are oily and also breaking out, salicylic acid covers both jobs in one step.

### Blackheads and whiteheads

Salicylic acid wins, decisively. Comedones are plugs of sebum and dead cells inside the follicle. Surface exfoliation barely touches them. The comparative peel evidence is consistent: salicylic acid outperforms on non inflammatory, comedonal lesions ([Dayal et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31553119/)). To understand which type of breakout you have, the AAD breaks down the [different types of acne](https://www.aad.org/public/diseases/acne/diy/types-breakouts).

### Dullness and rough texture

Glycolic acid wins. Surface resurfacing is exactly its strength. A 5 to 8 percent leave on glycolic acid two or three nights a week visibly smooths and brightens within a month.

### Fine lines and sun damage

Glycolic acid wins. By increasing epidermal turnover and supporting collagen friendly conditions, it gradually softens early lines and improves photoaged texture ([Edison et al., 2004](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/15002656/)). Salicylic acid does little here.

### Uneven tone and dark marks

Both help, glycolic acid leads. Glycolic acid lifts pigmented surface cells faster, so post inflammatory marks fade sooner. Salicylic acid contributes by clearing the breakouts that cause new marks in the first place. For stubborn pigmentation, layer glycolic acid into a routine built around the actives in our [hyperpigmentation serum guide](/blog/best-serums-hyperpigmentation-dark-spots/).

### Sensitive or reactive skin

Neither is the gentlest choice. Glycolic acid is the most likely of the two to sting and flush. Salicylic acid is gentler at 0.5 to 1 percent, but for genuinely reactive skin, lactic acid, a larger and slower AHA, is usually the kindest exfoliant. Patch test on the inner forearm for three nights before applying to the face.

## How to use them safely

Use the lowest effective strength, the lowest effective frequency, and never combine an acid with another strong active on the same night.

A safe starting protocol for either acid looks like this:

1. **Start at the bottom of the range.** Salicylic acid at 0.5 to 2 percent. Glycolic acid at 5 to 8 percent leave on. There is no prize for the highest number.
2. **Begin twice a week.** Apply in the evening to clean, dry skin. Wait until skin is fully dry after cleansing, especially with glycolic acid, because damp skin increases penetration and stinging.
3. **Buffer if you are sensitive.** Apply a plain moisturiser first, then the acid, then moisturiser again. This sandwich method slows delivery and reduces irritation.
4. **Build up slowly.** If skin stays calm after two weeks, move to three nights a week. Most people never need more.
5. **Protect the barrier.** Follow with a moisturiser containing ceramides. If you are not sure why that matters, our explainer on [ceramides and the skin barrier](/blog/ceramides-explained-why-your-barrier-needs-them/) covers it.
6. **Wear sunscreen every morning.** Both acids increase photosensitivity. SPF 30 or higher is mandatory, glycolic acid users especially.

### What never to layer the same evening

Do not stack salicylic acid or glycolic acid with any of these on the same night:

- **Retinol or retinoids.** Two strong actives at once means a wrecked barrier, not faster results. Alternate nights instead. New to retinol? Start with our [retinol beginners guide](/blog/retinol-beginners-guide-how-to-start/).
- **Vitamin C in its pure L ascorbic acid form.** The pH conflict reduces both and irritates skin.
- **Another exfoliating acid.** One acid per night is plenty. Doubling up is the fastest route to a damaged barrier.

## Can you use salicylic acid and glycolic acid together?

Yes, but on different nights, not in the same application. Some people genuinely benefit from both: salicylic acid to keep oily, congested zones clear, and glycolic acid to resurface and brighten. The safe way to run both is to alternate. For example, salicylic acid on Monday and Thursday, glycolic acid on Wednesday and Saturday, with rest nights between. Layering the two in one sitting overloads the skin and raises the irritation risk without improving results.

If your skin is oily in the T zone and dry on the cheeks, you can also multi mask the idea: salicylic acid only on the oily zones, glycolic acid only on the duller, drier areas. Keep the frequency conservative whenever you run two acids in one week.

## Which one should you buy?

Buy salicylic acid if your skin is oily, congested, breakout prone or studded with blackheads. Buy glycolic acid if your skin is normal to dry, dull, rough, sun damaged or unevenly toned. If you are not sure, or your skin is sensitive, start with a low percentage salicylic acid or a lactic acid product, both of which are more forgiving than glycolic acid.

Whatever you choose, judge the formula, not the marketing. Check the labelled concentration, the position of the acid in the INCI list, and whether the base supports the barrier with ceramides or niacinamide. ## Frequently asked questions

### Is salicylic acid or glycolic acid better for acne?

Salicylic acid is usually the better first choice for acne, especially congested and comedonal acne, because it is oil soluble and exfoliates inside the pore where the blockage forms. Comparative peel studies found salicylic acid particularly effective on non inflammatory lesions ([Dayal et al., 2020](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31553119/)). Glycolic acid can help acne prone skin too, but it works on the surface rather than inside the follicle.

### Can I use salicylic acid and glycolic acid together?

You can use both in the same week, but not in the same application. Alternate them on different nights with rest nights between, for example salicylic acid twice a week and glycolic acid twice a week. Layering both acids in one sitting overloads the skin and raises the risk of irritation and a damaged barrier without better results.

### Which exfoliant is gentler for sensitive skin?

Neither salicylic acid nor glycolic acid is the gentlest option for reactive skin. Glycolic acid is the most likely of the two to sting and cause redness. Low percentage salicylic acid (0.5 to 1 percent) is milder, but for genuinely sensitive skin, lactic acid is usually the kindest exfoliant because it is a larger, slower acting molecule. Always patch test first.

### Do I need sunscreen with these acids?

Yes, daily SPF 30 or higher is mandatory with both. Salicylic acid and glycolic acid increase your skin's sensitivity to ultraviolet light, and glycolic acid does so more noticeably. Skipping sunscreen while exfoliating can worsen the very dark spots and sun damage you may be trying to fade.

### How long until I see results?

Expect 2 to 4 weeks for salicylic acid to visibly reduce congestion and blackheads, and 2 to 6 weeks for glycolic acid to smooth texture and brighten. Fading of dark marks and improvement of fine lines takes longer, often 8 to 12 weeks of consistent use. Going faster by increasing strength or frequency usually causes irritation, not quicker progress.

## The bottom line

Salicylic acid and glycolic acid are not rivals so much as specialists. Salicylic acid is the oil soluble BHA that cleans out the pore, making it the right call for oily, congested and acne prone skin. Glycolic acid is the water soluble AHA that resurfaces the top of the skin, making it the right call for dull, rough, sun damaged and uneven toned skin. Choose by your dominant concern, start low and slow, protect your barrier, and never skip sunscreen. If you want both, alternate them across the week rather than stacking them. The smart routine is not the one with the strongest acid, it is the one matched to your skin.
