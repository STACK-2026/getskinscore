Mineral oil is the most maligned ingredient in skincare that almost every dermatologist still quietly recommends. It sits in the Nivea blue tin, in Aquaphor, in baby oil, and in countless drugstore moisturisers, yet clean beauty marketing has convinced shoppers it suffocates skin, clogs pores, and causes cancer. The science says none of that is true at the grade used in cosmetics.

> **TL;DR**: Cosmetic grade **mineral oil** (INCI: paraffinum liquidum) is a highly refined occlusive that reduces water loss through the skin by up to thirty percent. It is non comedogenic at cosmetic concentrations, classed as safe by the FDA, the CIR, and the EU, and judged by the WHO as "not classifiable" for cancer risk in its refined form. The horror stories come from **unrefined industrial grade oil**, which is never used in finished skincare. It is a workhorse occlusive, not a hero ingredient, and brands like Nivea use it because it works.

Sorting fact from fear matters because mineral oil is one of the few ingredients with decades of clinical use behind it, graded transparently on [SkinScore](/rankings/) by INCI list and concentration rather than by brand story.

## What is mineral oil and why has it become so controversial?

Mineral oil, listed on labels as paraffinum liquidum or paraffin oil, is a clear, odourless liquid distilled from crude petroleum and highly refined for cosmetic use. The refining strips out the polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons that make raw petroleum hazardous, leaving a chemically inert mixture of long chain saturated hydrocarbons that does not react with skin, does not oxidise, and does not feed bacteria.

It has been used in pharmacy and cosmetic formulation for more than a century. Vaseline, baby oil, Nivea Creme, Aquaphor, and most classic cold creams rely on it. The controversy is recent and driven by two threads. The first is "natural" and "clean" beauty positioning, which sells the absence of petroleum derivatives as a virtue. The second is the conflation of cosmetic grade mineral oil with the industrial lubricants and metalworking fluids that share a name but not a purity profile. A safe cosmetic ingredient inherited the reputation of a workshop solvent.

## The science: how mineral oil actually works on your skin

Mineral oil is an occlusive. Occlusives form a thin, hydrophobic film on the surface of the stratum corneum that slows transepidermal water loss, the constant evaporation of water from the deeper layers of skin into the air. Reduce TEWL and the skin holds onto its own moisture, which is the foundation of barrier repair.

Refined cosmetic mineral oil reduces TEWL by up to thirty percent, putting it in the same broad bracket as dimethicone and behind only petrolatum, which is the gold standard at around ninety nine percent occlusivity. Crucially, mineral oil does not penetrate the skin in any meaningful way. Its molecules are too large to pass through the stratum corneum, so it works at the surface and stays there. That is exactly what you want from an occlusive: a barrier that traps water without entering the bloodstream or interfering with skin chemistry.

Because it does not react with anything, mineral oil is also a stable base for cleansing oils and a non-irritating solvent for makeup removal. The same inertness that makes it boring makes it safe.

## Myth vs fact: debunking the five biggest lies about mineral oil

The myths around mineral oil are surprisingly few and surprisingly resilient. Each one falls apart under the smallest amount of scrutiny.

- **"Mineral oil is carcinogenic."** This is true of untreated, industrial grade mineral oil contaminated with polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons. It is not true of cosmetic grade oil, which the [World Health Organization](https://www.who.int/) classes as Group 3, "not classifiable as to its carcinogenicity to humans." The [US Food and Drug Administration](https://www.fda.gov/) permits it as a direct food additive, which is a higher bar than topical cosmetic use.
- **"Mineral oil clogs pores."** Decades of comedogenicity testing, including a frequently cited 2005 paper in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology, place cosmetic mineral oil at zero to two on the five point comedogenicity scale, the same range as squalane. The classic five point scale itself originated in rabbit ear testing and overstates risk in human skin, but even on its own terms cosmetic mineral oil does not score as a clogger.
- **"Mineral oil suffocates the skin."** Skin does not breathe. Gas exchange happens in the lungs. The stratum corneum is already a barrier; adding an occlusive on top does not stop oxygen reaching cells, because cells are not pulling oxygen from the air.
- **"Mineral oil prevents the skin from absorbing other actives."** Mineral oil is usually one phase of a finished formula, not a sealed lacquer. Modern moisturisers blend it with humectants, emollients, and water so that other actives still penetrate. Even as a final occlusive layer (slugging), it traps existing skincare against the skin rather than blocking it.
- **"Mineral oil dries the skin out underneath."** This is a circular myth. Occlusives reduce water loss, which is the opposite of drying. They do not add water to the skin, so a routine that uses only mineral oil with no humectant underneath will feel less hydrating, but that is a formulation gap, not a property of the ingredient.

## Cosmetic grade vs industrial grade: the purity that changes everything

The single most important distinction in any mineral oil conversation is grade. Cosmetic and pharmaceutical grade mineral oil is refined to remove the PAHs and other impurities that make crude petroleum products hazardous. The result is white mineral oil, USP grade, which meets pharmacopoeia standards for use on skin, in eyes, and even internally.

Industrial grade mineral oil, used as a machine lubricant or coolant, is a completely different material. It can contain PAHs and other contaminants tied to occupational skin cancer in long term, high exposure settings such as metalworking. Studies linking "mineral oil" to skin tumours trace back to these industrial exposures, not finished cosmetics.

Finished cosmetic regulations in the EU, the US, Japan, and South Korea all require cosmetic or pharmaceutical grade material. When you see paraffinum liquidum on the back of a Nivea tin or in a La Roche-Posay barrier balm, that is the refined material, not the workshop drum. The [Cosmetic Ingredient Review](https://www.cir-safety.org/) panel has reaffirmed the safety of refined mineral oil multiple times since 1984.

## Mineral oil vs the alternatives: a data backed comparison

Mineral oil is one of several occlusives a formulator can reach for. Each has its own trade-offs.

| Occlusive | TEWL reduction | Feel | Comedogenicity | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Petrolatum** | Up to 99 percent | Heavy, greasy | 0 | The reference standard for barrier repair. |
| **Mineral oil** | Up to 30 percent | Light, slippery | 0 to 2 | Inert, stable, well tolerated. |
| **Dimethicone** | Around 20 to 30 percent | Silky, dry finish | 0 | Cosmetically elegant silicone. |
| **Lanolin** | Around 20 to 30 percent | Tacky, waxy | 1 to 2 | Risk of allergy in sensitised users. |
| **Plant oils (jojoba, squalane)** | 5 to 20 percent | Cosmetic, dry | 0 to 2 | Mostly emollients; weaker occlusion. |
| **Shea butter** | Around 10 to 20 percent | Rich, heavy | 0 to 2 | Emollient with mild occlusion. |

Mineral oil's role is the workhorse middle ground: more elegant than petrolatum, more occlusive than most plant oils, cheaper and more stable than dimethicone. For very dry or eczema-prone skin, formulators pair it with petrolatum and humectants to combine the best of both. ## How to use mineral oil in your skincare routine

Mineral oil is best deployed as the occlusive seal at the end of a routine, not as the only step. The order that maximises its benefit looks like this.

1. **Cleanse** with a gentle, pH balanced cleanser. Our [best gentle cleanser guide](/blog/best-cleanser-sensitive-skin-gentle/) covers options that pair well with occlusive moisturisers.
2. **Apply a humectant** (a hyaluronic acid or glycerin serum) on damp skin so there is water in the upper layers for the occlusive to trap.
3. **Layer a moisturiser** with emollients and a few actives if you use them.
4. **Seal with a mineral oil based balm or cream**, especially at night and in winter, to lock in hydration and reduce overnight TEWL.

Mineral oil is also the unsung hero of cleansing oils and balms, where its inert lipophilic structure dissolves sunscreen, sebum, and waterproof makeup without irritation. ## Who should reach for mineral oil and who can skip it

Mineral oil earns a place in most barrier repair routines, but the best use cases line up clearly.

- **Dry, dehydrated, and mature skin** benefits most. The reduced TEWL is the lever, and a mineral oil based night cream is one of the cheapest, most effective barrier supports available. Our [best moisturiser for dry skin guide](/blog/best-moisturizer-dry-skin-dermatologist-picks/) features several mineral oil and petrolatum based options.
- **Eczema, atopic, and post-procedure skin** does well with mineral oil and petrolatum blends. The American Academy of Dermatology's atopic dermatitis guidance specifically calls out petrolatum and mineral oil based emollients as first line moisturisers, and a [PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/) literature search returns consistent support for their role in barrier repair.
- **Compromised barriers in winter or after actives** (retinoids, acids) benefit from a heavier seal. See our [ceramides explainer](/blog/ceramides-explained-why-your-barrier-needs-them/) for the actives that pair well underneath.
- **Pregnancy and infancy:** mineral oil and petrolatum are among the most established safe occlusives across both contexts. Our [pregnancy safe skincare guide](/blog/pregnancy-safe-skincare-ingredient-guide/) lists them alongside other green-lit ingredients.

Very oily and acne-prone users in humid climates can skip a thick mineral oil balm, not because the ingredient is dangerous but because the heavy occlusion is unnecessary. A lighter dimethicone gel or a humectant serum often serves that skin type better.

## The SkinScore verdict on mineral oil

On the SkinScore framework, mineral oil is a safe, well tolerated, broadly beneficial occlusive. It is not penalised in our grading model, and products that combine it with humectants, emollients, and well chosen actives routinely earn A and B grades. What we penalise is poor overall formulation: a mineral oil heavy product with no humectant, no antioxidant, and a heavy fragrance load is weaker than the same base paired with glycerin and niacinamide.

The ingredient is not the problem. The formula and your skin's needs are. A 4 euro tube of Nivea Creme can outperform a 90 euro luxury moisturiser if the rest of the formula serves your barrier better.

## Bottom line

Cosmetic grade mineral oil is one of the safest, most studied, and most reliable occlusives in skincare. It reduces water loss, supports barrier repair, does not clog pores at cosmetic concentrations, and has been cleared as safe by every major regulator that has reviewed it. The carcinogen scare belongs to industrial grade petroleum products that never enter a cosmetic factory. Brands like Nivea use mineral oil because it works, and a generation of dermatologists keeps recommending it because the evidence has not changed.

## Frequently asked questions

### Is mineral oil safe to use on your face?
Yes. Cosmetic grade mineral oil (paraffinum liquidum) is classed as safe by the FDA, the CIR, and the EU at the concentrations used in finished skincare. It is inert, does not penetrate the skin, does not react with cells, and has been used in cosmetic and pharmaceutical products for more than a century without credible evidence of harm on the face.

### Does mineral oil clog pores or cause acne?
Cosmetic mineral oil scores between zero and two on the five point comedogenicity scale, the same range as squalane, and a 2005 review in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology confirmed it as non comedogenic at the concentrations used in finished products. People who break out on a mineral oil cream are usually reacting to another ingredient in the formula, often a fragrance or a wax, not the oil itself.

### Is mineral oil in Nivea Creme bad for you?
No. Nivea Creme uses cosmetic grade paraffinum liquidum, the refined version that contains no detectable PAHs. The brand's formulation is one of the oldest continuously sold moisturisers in the world and is regulated by both EU and individual national cosmetic safety bodies. Its safety record is long and uncontroversial.

### Does mineral oil cause cancer?
There is no credible evidence that cosmetic grade mineral oil causes cancer. The cancer link comes from unrefined industrial grade oil exposed in workplaces such as metalworking, which contains PAHs. Refined cosmetic mineral oil is classified by the WHO as Group 3, "not classifiable as to its carcinogenicity to humans," and is approved by the FDA as a direct food additive.

### What is the difference between mineral oil and petrolatum?
Both are refined petroleum derivatives. Mineral oil is liquid at room temperature and reduces water loss by up to thirty percent. Petrolatum (Vaseline) is a semi solid that reduces water loss by up to ninety nine percent. Both are safe at cosmetic grade and are often combined in the same product to balance feel and occlusion.

### Should I avoid mineral oil if I have oily or combination skin?
You do not have to avoid it, but you may not need a mineral oil heavy product if your skin is oily. A lightweight gel moisturiser with dimethicone or a humectant serum often suits oily skin better than a thick mineral oil balm, especially in humid climates. For combination skin, a mineral oil based product applied to dry zones rather than the full face works well.
