Nivea Creme has sold for more than a hundred years on a formula that is mostly water, mineral oil and a wax stabiliser. Aquaphor, the ointment dermatologists hand out after laser and tattoo work, is built around petrolatum and mineral oil. Yet open any clean beauty blog and you will read that mineral oil is a toxic petroleum byproduct that suffocates the skin and may cause cancer. Both things cannot be true. Below is what the science actually says, why the confusion exists, and how to decide whether a mineral oil product belongs in your routine.

## TL;DR

**Cosmetic grade mineral oil is one of the safest and best studied occlusives in skincare.** It is highly refined white oil, listed on labels as paraffinum liquidum or mineral oil USP. The FDA, the Cosmetic Ingredient Review and the World Health Organization classify it as safe and non carcinogenic at the purity used in cosmetics. It is non comedogenic for most skin types, it reduces transepidermal water loss by around thirty percent, and it has a near zero allergy rate. The fear comes from industrial grade mineral oil, a different product with a different purity profile.

## What is mineral oil and why is it so controversial

Mineral oil is a clear, odourless liquid distilled from crude petroleum. The cosmetic grade used in skincare is what chemists call white oil: it goes through hydrotreating, sulfuric acid washing and clay filtration until almost every aromatic hydrocarbon, including the polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons that worry toxicologists, is removed. The result is one of the most chemically inert ingredients you can put on skin.

The controversy comes from two places. First, the clean beauty movement made petroleum derived ingredients a marketing villain regardless of safety data. Second, mineral oil exists in many grades, and food, pharmaceutical and cosmetic versions share a name with industrial lubricants that are not purified to the same level. Citing a study on motor oil and applying it to Nivea is a category error, but it happens constantly online.

## How mineral oil actually works on your skin

Mineral oil is an occlusive. It does not penetrate the stratum corneum because its molecules are too large and too hydrophobic. Instead, it forms a thin water resistant film on the surface that slows evaporation from the deeper layers. This is the same mechanism that makes petrolatum the gold standard occlusive in dermatology.

A [2007 paper in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17714466/) measured a roughly thirty percent reduction in transepidermal water loss after a single application of a mineral oil based moisturiser. That is a meaningful number for compromised barriers, which is why post procedure ointments, eczema creams and pharmacy lip balms still lean on mineral oil and petrolatum despite a hundred newer alternatives. ## Myth versus fact: the five biggest claims about mineral oil

**Myth 1: Mineral oil causes cancer.** The [World Health Organization](https://www.iarc.who.int/) classifies untreated and mildly treated mineral oils as Group 1 carcinogens, but the highly refined cosmetic grade is explicitly excluded from this classification. The [FDA](https://www.fda.gov/) approves mineral oil for use in food, pharmaceuticals and cosmetics without restriction, and the [Cosmetic Ingredient Review](https://www.cir-safety.org/) has reaffirmed cosmetic grade safety in multiple monographs. Confusing the two grades is the central error in nearly every alarmist article.

**Myth 2: It suffocates the skin.** Skin does not breathe. Gas exchange happens through the lungs and blood vessels, not through the stratum corneum. An occlusive film does not deprive cells of oxygen.

**Myth 3: It clogs pores.** Cosmetic grade mineral oil scores zero to one on the comedogenic scale used in dermatology research. A [2005 review in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/17129244/), and earlier work by DiNardo, both found no comedogenic effect in human use, a position the [American Academy of Dermatology](https://www.aad.org/) continues to support. If you want a broader breakdown of which ingredients actually clog pores, our [comedogenic ingredients list](/blog/comedogenic-ingredients-complete-list/) ranks the real offenders.

**Myth 4: It blocks other actives from working.** Mineral oil is usually the last step, applied over serums. It seals what is already there. If you layer it under a vitamin C or a retinol, the active still reaches the skin because most actives are formulated to absorb in the first few minutes after application. Our [routine order guide](/blog/skincare-routine-order-morning-night/) covers the layering rules.

**Myth 5: It leaches vitamins from the body.** This claim comes from a misreading of old studies on oral mineral oil used as a laxative. Topical application does not enter the bloodstream in measurable amounts.

## Mineral oil versus the alternatives

Choosing an occlusive is rarely about safety, since most are safe. It is about feel, sustainability and cost.

**Petrolatum** is the strongest occlusive available, reducing water loss by around ninety nine percent. It is also the heaviest and stickiest. Mineral oil is essentially a lighter, more spreadable cousin.

**Silicones** like dimethicone give a similar silky finish with less greasiness and more breathability. They are a frequent substitute in modern formulas, and we cover them in detail in the [silicones safety review](/blog/silicones-skincare-safe-dimethicone/).

**Plant oils** such as squalane, jojoba or argan are marketed as natural alternatives. Squalane is the closest in feel and stability. Most other plant oils oxidise faster, vary by harvest, and carry a real allergy risk that mineral oil simply does not have.

**Shea butter and lanolin** are richer emollients than occlusives. Lanolin in particular is potent for cracked skin but carries a higher sensitisation rate than mineral oil.

The honest summary: if you tolerate mineral oil, there is no clinical reason to pay three times as much for a botanical occlusive that performs the same job.

## Who should and should not use mineral oil

Mineral oil suits dry, mature, eczema prone and post procedure skin. It is a frequent ingredient in pharmacy classics like CeraVe Healing Ointment, Aquaphor and the original Nivea Creme. For a wider look at moisturisers in this category, our [best moisturisers for dry skin](/blog/best-moisturizer-dry-skin-dermatologist-picks/) feature ranks several mineral oil formulas highly.

Oily and acne prone skin can usually tolerate it too, but the heavy occlusion may feel uncomfortable in hot climates or on already congested skin. If you find your face feels weighed down, a lighter humectant heavy moisturiser is a better pick.

The slugging trend, which involves coating the face in a thin layer of petrolatum or mineral oil overnight, is the clearest modern use case. We dig into the evidence and the risks in our [slugging review](/blog/slugging-vaseline-trend-dermatologist-opinion/).

## How to read a label and spot mineral oil

On an INCI list, mineral oil can appear under several names. The most common are mineral oil, paraffinum liquidum, paraffin oil, white oil and petrolatum liquid. All are the same family of ingredients at cosmetic purity. The position on the list matters: an ingredient in the top five is usually present at a meaningful percentage. Toward the bottom it is likely a trace stabiliser.

European labels almost always use paraffinum liquidum. American labels favour mineral oil. Both are regulated by their respective authorities, and both must meet pharmacopoeial purity standards when sold as cosmetics or over the counter drugs.

## The SkinScore verdict

Mineral oil is a low cost, low allergy, high performance occlusive that has been used safely for a century. The cancer claims confuse cosmetic grade with industrial grade. The pore clogging claims are not supported by the comedogenic studies. The suffocation claim misunderstands basic physiology. If you have a dry, irritated or compromised barrier, mineral oil is one of the most evidence supported ingredients you can pick, and pharmacy classics built around it remain better value than most luxury alternatives. If you are oily, acne prone or simply do not like the feel, plenty of other occlusives will do the same job.

## FAQ

**Is mineral oil safe for the face?**
Yes, cosmetic grade mineral oil is rated safe by the FDA, the Cosmetic Ingredient Review panel and the European Scientific Committee on Consumer Safety. It is used in pharmacy moisturisers, post procedure ointments and lip balms because of its safety profile, not despite it.

**Does mineral oil cause cancer?**
No, not at the purity used in cosmetics. The [International Agency for Research on Cancer](https://www.iarc.who.int/) classifies untreated industrial mineral oils as Group 1 carcinogens, but explicitly excludes highly refined cosmetic and food grade oil from this classification. The American Academy of Dermatology has repeatedly affirmed cosmetic mineral oil is safe.

**Does mineral oil clog pores?**
For the vast majority of users, no. It scores zero to one on the five point comedogenic scale used in dermatology research, and a 2005 study in the Journal of Cosmetic Dermatology found no comedogenic activity in cosmetic grade samples. Individuals with very oily or acne prone skin may still prefer lighter alternatives by feel.

**What is the difference between mineral oil and petrolatum?**
They come from the same petroleum refining process. Petrolatum is the semisolid version, mineral oil is the liquid version. Petrolatum is a stronger occlusive but heavier on the skin. Mineral oil spreads more easily and gives a lighter finish.

**Is paraffinum liquidum the same as mineral oil?**
Yes. Paraffinum liquidum is the INCI name used on European labels for cosmetic grade mineral oil. American labels use the name mineral oil. They are the same ingredient at the same purity standard.

**Why do clean beauty brands avoid mineral oil?**
Mostly for marketing reasons. Petroleum derived ingredients sell poorly in the clean beauty category regardless of safety data. There are also legitimate environmental questions about petroleum sourcing, but the human safety case against cosmetic grade mineral oil is not supported by current evidence from the FDA, the European Scientific Committee or peer reviewed dermatology research.
