Few ingredients carry a reputation as split as mineral oil. To a dermatologist it is one of the safest, most studied, most boring substances you can put on skin. To a clean beauty influencer it is a "cheap by-product of petroleum" that smothers your face and may even cause cancer. The same molecule sits in the blue tin of Nivea Creme that your grandmother used and in the "toxic ingredients to avoid" lists that fill social media. Both stories cannot be true, and the gap between them is one of the clearest examples of how marketing rewrites chemistry.

This guide explains what mineral oil and petrolatum actually are, why formulators have leaned on them for over a century, whether they are safe, where the cancer scare came from, and why the pore-clogging claim falls apart under testing. Every product reference on [SkinScore](/rankings/) is graded on its full INCI list, not on whether a marketing team decided to chase a "mineral oil free" tag.

> **TL;DR**: Mineral oil, listed as paraffinum liquidum, and its thicker cousin petrolatum are occlusives. They sit on the skin surface and slow water loss rather than feeding the skin anything. Cosmetic grade mineral oil is one of the most purified ingredients in any formula, and the FDA, the Cosmetic Ingredient Review panel, and European regulators consider it safe and non-comedogenic. The cancer scare comes from unrefined industrial grades, not the cosmetic grade in your moisturiser. The pore-clogging story is not supported by comedogenicity testing. The honest verdict is that mineral oil is safe and effective at one narrow job, sealing in water, and adds nothing active beyond that.

## What is mineral oil in skincare? A simple definition

Mineral oil is a clear, odourless, tasteless liquid made of saturated hydrocarbons derived from crude oil. That last word is what triggers the alarm, so it helps to understand the process. Crude oil is a chaotic mix of thousands of compounds. To turn part of it into cosmetic grade mineral oil, refiners distil it, then treat it with hydrogen and filter out the aromatic and reactive fractions until what remains is a narrow band of stable, inert, long chain alkanes. The result has more in common with a purified laboratory reagent than with anything you would call petrol.

On a label, mineral oil appears most often as **paraffinum liquidum**, the INCI name used across Europe. You will also see it written as mineral oil, liquid paraffin, or paraffin oil. Its semi-solid relative, **petrolatum** (petroleum jelly, the active in Vaseline), is the same family of hydrocarbons in a thicker, waxier form. Both do the same fundamental thing on skin, and both have the same safety story.

The key idea is that mineral oil is an **occlusive**, not a moisturiser in the way most people mean. It does not add water, it does not feed the skin nutrients, and it does not penetrate into living tissue. It forms a thin, water repelling film on the very top layer and slows the rate at which water evaporates out of the skin. That single property explains almost everything about how it behaves, the good and the limited.

## How occlusives work: the science of sealing in water

Your skin constantly loses water from the inside out, a process called transepidermal water loss, or TEWL. A healthy skin barrier keeps that loss low. A compromised barrier, the kind you get with eczema, over-exfoliation, harsh cleansing, or winter air, lets too much water escape, and the skin feels tight, rough, and reactive. Occlusives work by laying a hydrophobic layer over the surface that physically slows evaporation, giving the barrier underneath time to recover.

Petrolatum is the benchmark here. It reduces TEWL by more than ninety percent in lab measurements, far more than any plant oil, which is why dermatologists reach for it on cracked hands, chapped lips, and post-procedure skin. Mineral oil is lighter and seals less aggressively, which makes it more cosmetically pleasant in a daily face cream while still cutting water loss meaningfully. A study using confocal Raman microspectroscopy showed that occlusion physically [changes the water bonding state in the stratum corneum](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/32692411/), the outermost skin layer, hydrating it from within rather than from added water on top.

This is the same logic behind the slugging trend, where people seal their face with a thin layer of petrolatum overnight. If you want the full breakdown of that practice and when it makes sense, our guide on [the slugging trend and what dermatologists think](/blog/slugging-vaseline-trend-dermatologist-opinion/) covers the evidence in detail. The short version is that occlusion is a real, well-documented mechanism, and mineral oil is one of the cleanest ways to achieve it.

It is worth being clear about what occlusion does not do. Sealing water in is not the same as actively repairing the barrier. For that you want barrier lipids the skin actually uses, which is where ceramides come in. Our piece on [why your skin barrier needs ceramides](/blog/ceramides-explained-why-your-barrier-needs-them/) explains the difference between a sealant that holds water in and an ingredient that rebuilds the wall itself. The best barrier creams usually combine both: an occlusive to stop the leak and barrier lipids to fix the structure.

## Cosmetic grade vs industrial grade: the distinction that ends the cancer scare

This is the single most important section, because almost every fear about mineral oil collapses once you separate two completely different products that share a name.

**Industrial grade mineral oils**, the kind used as machine lubricants, cutting fluids, and in some heavy manufacturing, are minimally refined. They retain polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), some of which are genuinely carcinogenic. Occupational studies of workers exposed to unrefined oils over decades, by skin contact and inhalation, do show raised cancer risk. The International Agency for Research on Cancer classifies **untreated and mildly treated** mineral oils as carcinogenic to humans. That classification is real, and it is the source of every "mineral oil causes cancer" headline.

**Cosmetic grade mineral oil** is a different material. It is refined to pharmaceutical and food standards, with the aromatic and reactive fractions stripped out to trace levels regulated in parts per million. The same IARC review explicitly classifies **highly refined** mineral oils as not classifiable as a carcinogen, because the refining removes the components that drive the risk. The mineral oil in a Nivea cream, a baby oil, or a lip balm is this highly refined grade, held to purity limits that industrial lubricants never meet.

Confusing the two is like reading that crude petroleum is toxic and concluding that purified petroleum jelly, sold in pharmacies for chapped lips and used on newborns, is dangerous. The starting material is shared, the finished product is not. Regulators on both sides of the Atlantic, including the [Cosmetic Ingredient Review panel](https://www.cir-safety.org/ingredients) in the United States and the European authorities behind the [CosIng cosmetic ingredient database](https://ec.europa.eu/growth/tools-databases/cosing/), assess cosmetic mineral oil on its actual purity, and both conclude it is safe as used. For the closest analogue of how a benzene-style contamination scare differs from an inherent ingredient risk, the American Cancer Society's overview of [benzene and cancer risk](https://www.cancer.org/cancer/risk-prevention/chemicals/benzene.html) is a useful reference point on why contamination grade, not ingredient name, is what matters.

## The pore-clogging myth: is mineral oil comedogenic?

The second big fear is that mineral oil clogs pores and triggers breakouts. The word people reach for is **comedogenic**, meaning it forms comedones, the plugs that become blackheads and whiteheads. The claim sounds plausible because mineral oil is, well, an oil, and oily skin already feels prone to congestion. The evidence does not support it.

Most comedogenicity ratings still circulating online trace back to the **rabbit ear assay** from the 1970s and 1980s, where ingredients were applied in high concentration to rabbit ears and the follicles examined for plugging. Researchers later showed the model is a poor predictor of what happens on human skin, because rabbit ear follicles react far more strongly than human pores to occlusion. A 1982 review in the Journal of the American Academy of Dermatology asked directly [whether the rabbit ear model predicts acne in people](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/6461674/) and found the correlation weak. Many ingredients flagged as comedogenic on rabbit ears do not cause comedones on human faces.

When cosmetic grade mineral oil is tested on human skin, it scores at or near zero on the comedogenic scale. It is chemically inert, it is not metabolised by the skin or by the bacteria involved in acne, and it does not oxidise the way some plant oils do. By contrast, several "natural" oils marketed as cleaner alternatives sit higher on the comedogenic scale and carry a real contact dermatitis risk, a point reviewed in the literature on [natural oils and contact dermatitis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/27373890/). This pattern of an old, inert ingredient being blamed for breakouts mirrors the silicone debate almost exactly. The same "it suffocates your skin" language gets applied to dimethicone, and the same testing reality undercuts it. ## Mineral oil vs plant oils: which is actually better for your skin?

The "natural is better" instinct pushes people from mineral oil toward plant oils like coconut, argan, rosehip, or sunflower. As a barrier ingredient, this swap is often a downgrade, and the reasons are worth understanding.

Plant oils are chemically complex. They contain unsaturated fatty acids, plant sterols, vitamin E, and trace proteins, and that complexity is a double-edged sword. The unsaturated bonds make plant oils prone to oxidation, which can generate irritating breakdown products over time. The plant proteins and natural fragrance compounds are a genuine source of allergic contact dermatitis. And the fatty acid profile matters: oils high in oleic acid, such as olive oil, can actually disrupt the skin barrier rather than support it. A controlled trial comparing [olive and sunflower oil on the adult skin barrier](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22995032/) found that olive oil damaged barrier function even in volunteers without sensitive skin.

Mineral oil sidesteps all of this. It has no unsaturated bonds to oxidise, no proteins to trigger allergy, and no fragrance. That inertness is exactly why it is the standard base for sensitive skin, baby products, and post-procedure care. The trade-off is that it brings nothing extra. Plant oils, used well, can deliver antioxidants and barrier lipids that mineral oil cannot. Neither is universally better. The right choice depends on what the formula is trying to do.

| Property | Cosmetic mineral oil | Typical plant oil |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Occlusive strength | High and consistent | Variable, usually lower |
| Oxidation risk | None | Moderate to high |
| Allergy / irritation risk | Very low | Higher (proteins, fragrance) |
| Active benefits | None beyond sealing | Possible antioxidants, fatty acids |
| Barrier-disrupting risk | None | Some (high-oleic oils) |
| Best use | Sensitive skin, sealing, baby care | Targeted nourishment, when tolerated |

The practical takeaway is that mineral oil is the safer default for sealing and protecting, while a well-chosen plant oil can add value when the skin tolerates it. For dry skin specifically, the strongest formulas tend to pair an occlusive with humectants and barrier lipids rather than relying on any single oil, which is the logic behind our [dermatologist picks for dry skin moisturisers](/blog/best-moisturizer-dry-skin-dermatologist-picks/).

## Why Nivea, baby oil, and pharmacy creams still use it

If mineral oil is so demonised in marketing, why do trusted, decades-old products keep using it? Because it solves real problems cheaply and reliably, with a safety record almost no newer ingredient can match.

The blue tin of Nivea Creme, launched in 1911, is built around mineral oil and a wax-based emulsifier. It works because the occlusive film it leaves is stable, non-irritating, and effective at holding moisture in dry climates. Baby oil is almost pure mineral oil with a little fragrance, chosen precisely because the base is inert enough for infant skin. Pharmacy emollients prescribed for eczema lean on mineral oil and petrolatum because the [American Academy of Dermatology's advice on relieving dry skin](https://www.aad.org/public/everyday-care/skin-care-basics/dry/dermatologists-tips-relieve-dry-skin) centres on occlusion, and these ingredients deliver it without the allergy risk of botanicals.

There is also a manufacturing reason. Mineral oil is colourless, odourless, stable for years, and does not go rancid, which makes it easy to formulate, easy to preserve, and forgiving to store. For a mass-market product that needs to behave the same in Lagos and in Helsinki, that consistency is a feature, not a corner cut.

None of this makes mineral oil a hero ingredient. It is a workhorse. The criticism that it is "just a barrier" is accurate, but a barrier is exactly what a lot of skin needs, and there are worse things in skincare than an ingredient that does one safe job well.

## How to read a label and decide for yourself

The clean beauty movement turned mineral oil into a villain partly because "free from" labels are easy to sell and chemistry is hard to explain. The same playbook gave us the paraben scare, where a safe, well-studied preservative was replaced by alternatives that were sometimes worse. If that pattern interests you, our breakdown of [whether parabens are actually safe](/blog/parabens-safe-science-truth/) shows the same gap between marketing and evidence.

When you read an INCI list, treat **paraffinum liquidum** and **petrolatum** as what they are: safe, inert occlusives. Their position in the list tells you how much occlusion the product delivers. High up means a heavy, sealing texture suited to very dry or compromised skin. Low down means a light finish. Neither placement is a red flag.

The questions worth asking are about the whole formula, not the presence of one ingredient. Does the product pair the occlusive with humectants like glycerin and hyaluronic acid to draw water in, and barrier lipids like ceramides to rebuild the wall? Is it fragranced, which matters far more for sensitive skin than whether the oil is mineral or plant derived? A product is good or bad because of how its ingredients work together, which is exactly how every product on [SkinScore](/rankings/) is graded. A single ingredient, mineral oil included, almost never decides the verdict.

## The bottom line on mineral oil

Mineral oil is one of the most unfairly maligned ingredients in skincare. The fears do not survive contact with the evidence. The cancer scare belongs to unrefined industrial grades, not the pharmaceutical grade in your moisturiser. The pore-clogging claim rests on a discredited rabbit ear test. The "it suffocates skin" line misunderstands what occlusion is. What remains is a safe, inert, highly effective sealant that has protected dry and damaged skin for over a century.

It is not a treatment and never pretended to be. It will not deliver actives, antioxidants, or barrier lipids. But for the narrow job of stopping water from leaving the skin, it is among the cleanest, best-studied options available, and a "mineral oil free" label tells you nothing useful about whether a product is good.

## FAQ

### Is mineral oil bad for your skin?

No. Cosmetic grade mineral oil, listed as paraffinum liquidum, is one of the most refined and inert ingredients in skincare. It is considered safe and non-comedogenic by the FDA, the Cosmetic Ingredient Review panel, and European regulators. It does not penetrate living skin, does not feed bacteria, and does not oxidise. It is a sealant that slows water loss, and at that job it is both safe and effective.

### Does mineral oil cause cancer?

The highly refined mineral oil used in cosmetics is not classified as a carcinogen. The cancer concern applies to untreated and mildly refined industrial mineral oils, which retain polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons and were linked to cancer in occupational studies. Refining to cosmetic and pharmaceutical standards removes those components, which is why the same regulatory reviews that flag industrial grades clear the cosmetic grade.

### Does mineral oil clog pores and cause acne?

On human skin, cosmetic grade mineral oil scores at or near zero for comedogenicity. The "it clogs pores" reputation comes from the old rabbit ear test, which overstates pore-blocking compared with real human skin and is a poor predictor of acne. Mineral oil is inert and does not feed the bacteria involved in breakouts.

### What is the difference between mineral oil and petrolatum?

They are the same family of refined hydrocarbons in different physical forms. Mineral oil (paraffinum liquidum) is a clear liquid that seals lightly and feels cosmetically pleasant. Petrolatum (petroleum jelly, the base of Vaseline) is a thicker, waxy semi-solid that reduces water loss by over ninety percent. Both are safe occlusives, with petrolatum sealing more aggressively.

### Is mineral oil or plant oil better for skin?

Neither is universally better. Mineral oil is more inert, does not oxidise, and carries almost no allergy risk, which makes it ideal for sensitive skin, baby care, and barrier repair. Plant oils can add antioxidants and barrier lipids when the skin tolerates them, but they oxidise, can trigger allergy, and some high-oleic oils such as olive oil can disrupt the barrier. The best formulas often combine an occlusive with humectants and ceramides rather than relying on any single oil.

### Should I avoid products with mineral oil?

There is no evidence-based reason to avoid cosmetic mineral oil. A "mineral oil free" label tells you what is missing, not whether the product is good. Judge a product by its whole formula: whether it pairs occlusives with humectants and barrier lipids, and whether the fragrance and overall composition suit your skin. Mineral oil itself is one of the safest ingredients you can apply.
